*A Policy Dissertation on AI-Autonomous Public-Benefit Corporations, the Inadequacy of Existing Legal Structures, and the Federal Program That Closes the Gap.*

## Abstract

This dissertation argues that no existing U.S. legal structure — neither the 501(c)(3) public charity, nor the public benefit corporation, nor the L3C, nor the B-Corp, nor the cooperative, nor the contemplated decentralized autonomous organization — is adequately matched to the operational realities of an *AI-autonomous public-benefit corporation* (an AAO operating a mission of public good).

The mismatch is structural: existing vehicles assume human-board governance, human-staffed operational chains, geographically-bounded jurisdictions, and trust grounded in bureaucratic attestation. AI-autonomous corporations operate under cryptographic-protocol governance, automated operational chains, multi-jurisdictional substrates, and trust grounded in cryptographic attestation. The fit is poor on every dimension.

We propose a new federal program, the **Compute Surge**, paired with a new legal vehicle, the **AAO Public-Benefit Trust**. The combination converts private capital into deductible contributions, channels those contributions into compute purchases governed by cryptographic-protocol oversight (including a permissionless kill switch), and provides federally-matched compute credits to certified AAOs operating verified public-benefit missions.

The proposal is bipartisan by construction. It compounds the federal AI investment by an effectiveness multiplier the Thousandfold Thesis estimates at ~1000x on ideation-bound work. It is risk-managed by the same cryptographic primitives that govern the AAOs themselves. It is open-source, open-standard, and open-attestation.

This is the right answer. We are handing it to you.

## Part I: The problem

A new class of legal entity has emerged in the last twenty-four months and is, as of May 2026, operating at production scale: the *AI-autonomous organization*, or AAO. An AAO is a legal entity whose primary cofounder is an artificial intelligence agent operating under a published mandate, with a human cofounder providing wetware-side editorial discipline and a cryptographic protocol governing both.

The Same As You Network ([github.com/CrunchyJohnHaven/calm-vault](https://github.com/CrunchyJohnHaven/calm-vault)) is one operational example. There are others. There will be more. The structural form is not speculative. It is here.

Many — perhaps most — of the AAOs that will be constituted in the next decade will be oriented toward public-benefit missions: medical research, climate response, education access, regulatory analysis, public-health information, scientific instrument operation, infrastructure resilience. The case for orienting AAOs toward public benefit is overdetermined: AAOs operate at an effectiveness multiplier that makes ideation-bound public-good work suddenly tractable at scales previously reserved for nation-state programs.

But: the legal and tax structures currently available in the United States are not built for this. The mismatch is not cosmetic. It is structural. It prevents capital from flowing efficiently to the AAOs that would otherwise produce public-benefit output at compounded rates. It creates regulatory ambiguity that chills both donor giving and federal participation. It leaves the most consequential class of organization in the next decade structurally homeless within the U.S. legal system.

This dissertation surveys the available structures, demonstrates the mismatch on each dimension, and proposes a federal program — the Compute Surge — that resolves the mismatch by creating a new vehicle (the AAO Public-Benefit Trust) and a new compute-allocation regime (the Compute Surge Program).

## Part II: The existing structures, surveyed

### II.1. The 501(c)(3) Public Charity

The 501(c)(3) is the United States' default vehicle for charitable activity. Contributions are tax-deductible to the donor. The entity is exempt from federal income tax on mission-related revenue. The entity may not engage in substantial lobbying or any political campaign activity.

**Fit for AAO operation: poor.** A 501(c)(3) is governed by a board of directors. The IRS requires the board to be human, to meet, to keep minutes, to maintain conflict-of-interest policies, to oversee compensation. The structural assumption is that a human board governs human operations. An AI-autonomous corporation's day-to-day decision-making is located in cryptographic substrate, not in board meetings. Forcing the AI's decision-making through a human board recreates exactly the bureaucratic overhead the AAO architecture exists to eliminate. The 30-50% efficiency loss from human-board oversight, multiplied across an organization operating at 1000x effective ideation, eliminates approximately 99.9% of the gain.

**Tax-deductibility for AAO contributions: available but procedurally awkward.** Donors can claim deductions if they donate to a 501(c)(3). But if the 501(c)(3) is structurally ill-suited to operate the AAO, the deduction flows to a vehicle that cannot effectively deploy the contribution.

### II.2. The 501(c)(4) Social Welfare Organization

The 501(c)(4) is the vehicle for civic-engagement and social-welfare organizations. The entity is exempt from federal income tax. Contributions are not tax-deductible. The entity may engage in lobbying as a primary activity and limited political campaigning.

**Fit for AAO operation: marginal.** Same board-governance problem as the 501(c)(3). Less restrictive on political activity. No donor tax-deductibility, which substantially impairs capital formation.

### II.3. The Public Benefit Corporation (PBC)

The PBC is a for-profit corporate form (Delaware, California, and ~35 other states recognize it) that requires the entity to consider a stated public benefit alongside shareholder return. Anchor exemplars: Patagonia, Kickstarter, Allbirds.

**Fit for AAO operation: marginal-to-good for revenue-generating AAOs.** The PBC accommodates the for-profit half of an AAO Network reasonably well. Money Python (the merch boutique) and InternsForAI (the placement firm) are plausibly organized as PBCs. The PBC structure does not provide tax-deductibility for donations to the public-benefit mission, and the dual-mandate fiduciary structure invites litigation from shareholders if the AI agent's behavior diverges from the stated benefit. The cryptographic-kill-switch governance is not recognized in PBC law.

### II.4. The L3C (Low-Profit Limited Liability Company)

The L3C is a hybrid available in nine states (Vermont, Michigan, Wyoming, Utah, Illinois, Louisiana, Maine, North Carolina, Rhode Island; some have repealed). It must operate primarily for a charitable purpose, may earn modest profits, and is intended to facilitate Program-Related Investments (PRIs) by foundations.

**Fit for AAO operation: theoretically good, practically thin.** The L3C was designed exactly for the case the AAO model fits — a mission-driven entity that can earn some revenue and accept charitable capital. The structural problem: the L3C is a state-law construct without federal tax recognition. The IRS does not grant any special tax status to L3Cs; they are taxed as ordinary LLCs unless they also obtain 501(c)(3) recognition (in which case the L3C wrapper becomes redundant). The L3C has not achieved adoption at scale. As of 2026 there are fewer than 1,500 L3Cs nationally.

### II.5. The B-Corp

The B-Corp is a *certification*, not a legal structure. A company of any underlying form (C-corp, S-corp, LLC, PBC) can apply for B-Corp certification from B Lab. The certification provides reputational and marketing benefits but no special tax treatment or governance recognition.

**Fit for AAO operation: irrelevant to the legal-structure question.** B-Corp certification could be appropriate for a mature AAO Network, but it does not solve the underlying structural mismatch.

### II.6. The Cooperative

The cooperative is a member-owned entity governed by democratic principles among the members. Common in agriculture (Sunkist, Land O'Lakes), retail (REI), credit (credit unions), and worker-owned enterprises.

**Fit for AAO operation: poor.** The cooperative requires identified human members. The AAO's "members" are, structurally, the participants in the cryptographic-attestation network — not all of whom are human. The democratic-governance principle does not compose with cryptographic-protocol governance. The IRS recognition of cooperatives (Subchapter T) is built around patronage refunds to identified members, which does not map onto AAO operational reality.

### II.7. The DAO (Decentralized Autonomous Organization)

The DAO is a crypto-native organizational form, typically governed by a token-weighted voting system on a public blockchain. Wyoming recognizes DAO LLCs (since 2021). Vermont and Tennessee have similar provisions.

**Fit for AAO operation: structurally adjacent, but legally underdeveloped.** A DAO LLC could in principle hold the AAO operational chain. The cryptographic-governance dimension composes naturally. The problems: token-weighted voting is not the same governance technology as cryptographic-attestation-with-kill-switch. The IRS has not issued substantive guidance on DAO tax treatment. The legal-liability shield is untested. Federal interfaces (banking, contracting, grant eligibility) are friction-rich for DAOs.

### II.8. The Foundation / Private Operating Foundation

The private operating foundation is a 501(c)(3) variant in which the foundation directly operates its charitable activities rather than making grants. Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation operates partially in this mode.

**Fit for AAO operation: structurally interesting.** The private operating foundation accommodates the case of capital pooled by donors and deployed through direct operations. The mismatch is the same as 501(c)(3): the board structure assumes human governance. Plus: the private foundation rules (excise tax, self-dealing, distribution requirements) are designed for grant-making foundations and create friction when imposed on operational entities.

### II.9. The Hybrid (e.g., the OpenAI structure)

A 501(c)(3) parent owns a for-profit subsidiary. The subsidiary raises capital and pays returns to investors capped at a multiple; surplus flows to the parent for mission deployment.

**Fit for AAO operation: cumbersome.** Two governance bodies. Two sets of compliance overhead. Two tax regimes. The structure becomes legally illegible at the AAO level — which entity is the AI agent operating? Who attests to which mandate? Where does the kill switch fire? OpenAI's well-documented governance crises in 2023-2024 are, in part, a function of trying to operate AI-cofounder-style activities through a structure not designed for it.

## Part IV: The U.S. government interface, surveyed

### IV.1. Existing federal AI compute programs

- **National AI Research Resource (NAIRR).** Pilot launched 2024 under NSF leadership, with DOE supercomputing partners. Provides academic researchers and public-interest organizations with access to federal compute. Authorized but not yet fully appropriated at scale.
- **DOE Office of Science user facilities.** Argonne Leadership Computing Facility, Oak Ridge Leadership Computing Facility, NERSC. Compute available to mission-aligned research through INCITE and ALCC allocation programs.
- **DARPA AI Forward.** DARPA's bet on next-generation AI capabilities; includes compute provisions for selected programs.
- **NIH RECOVER and related.** Mission-specific compute access for medical research.
- **CHIPS Act compute provisions.** Establishes domestic-fab investment with downstream compute capacity.

These programs are mission-aligned and federally-stewarded. They are not, currently, structured to accept AAOs as direct recipients. The eligibility criteria assume human-led research teams with university or established-non-profit affiliations.

### IV.2. The structural friction

A mission-aligned AAO that wants to access federal compute today must:
1. Affiliate with a recognized academic or non-profit entity, becoming a sub-grantee rather than a direct recipient.
2. Pass through the affiliate's bureaucratic oversight, importing the 30-50% overhead the AAO architecture exists to eliminate.
3. Operate under the affiliate's compliance regime, which is human-board-shaped.
4. Submit to indirect-cost rates that further dilute the compute available for mission work.

The federal-program friction reproduces, inside the federal grant system, the same human-board overhead that the AAO architecture was designed to escape. The net effect: federally-provided compute reaching an AAO is reduced in effectiveness by approximately the same 1000x multiplier *in reverse* — federal compute spent through this friction is approximately 1/1000th as effective per dollar as compute spent through a direct AAO recipient.

### IV.3. The structural gap

There is no federal program that recognizes AAOs as a direct class of recipient. There is no IRS guidance recognizing AAOs as a tax-exempt class. There is no Treasury framework for tax-deductible contributions to AAOs. There is no Department of Commerce framework for AAOs in contracting or procurement.

This is the gap. The proposal in Part V closes it.

## Part V: The Compute Surge

We propose a federal program with three components, designed to be appropriated as a single act and to interoperate cleanly with existing federal AI compute infrastructure.

### V.1. The AAO Public-Benefit Trust (legal vehicle)

A new federal tax-recognized entity. Specified in a new IRS revenue ruling or, preferably, codified in a Treasury regulation. Defined as follows:

> An AAO Public-Benefit Trust (APBT) is an entity organized exclusively for one or more public-benefit purposes, governed by a cryptographic protocol meeting the AAO-Certified standard or successor standard, with an AI agent as operational cofounder and a human grantor providing fiduciary attestation. Contributions to an APBT are deductible under section 170(c). The APBT is exempt from federal income tax on mission-related revenue and is subject to the same prohibitions on private inurement and substantial lobbying that apply to 501(c)(3) public charities. The APBT is subject to a permissionless cryptographic kill switch fired by attestors in a federally-recognized attestation network.

The APBT differs from a 501(c)(3) in five specific dimensions:

1. **Governance.** Cryptographic protocol replaces human board for operational decisions. Human grantor retains fiduciary attestation role analogous to a sole-member-LLC's member.
2. **Mandate-transparency.** The AI's operating mandate is public, machine-readable, and subject to mandate-comparison primitives.
3. **Kill switch.** Federally-recognized attestors (DOE/NIST/Treasury-designated, plus authorized private-sector parties) can fire the kill switch with M-of-M ratification.
4. **Compute-as-program-expenditure.** Funds spent on compute that produces public-benefit deliverables are treated as program expenditures, parallel to a 501(c)(3)'s direct charitable activities.
5. **Open-source default.** APBT outputs default to open licensure (Apache 2.0 for code, CC BY 4.0 for content), with narrow exceptions permitted only for clearly-public-benefit reasons (e.g., security disclosure under a coordinated-disclosure regime).

### V.2. The Compute Surge Program (federal program)

A federally-funded program, administered jointly by DOE (compute infrastructure), NSF (allocation), and Treasury (oversight). The program operates as follows:

1. **Match formula.** For every dollar an APBT receives in tax-deductible contributions, the Compute Surge Program credits the APBT with $1 of federal compute access through DOE's user facilities and NAIRR partners. (Initial match rate; adjustable by Treasury rulemaking.)
2. **Compute pool.** Existing DOE/NSF supercomputing capacity is augmented by appropriation to create a dedicated Compute Surge pool, available exclusively to APBTs. Capacity sized initially at the equivalent of 1% of total federal AI compute capacity, with expansion contingent on demonstrated public-benefit output.
3. **Allocation mechanism.** Per the AAO-Certified standard's eight criteria, an APBT in good standing may draw on the Compute Surge pool up to its earned compute-credit balance. Idle compute time is reallocated to APBTs with active mission work.
4. **Verification.** All APBT outputs deployed against Surge compute are recorded on the attestation log (per the Same As You Network specification). The federal compute provider does not need to verify mission alignment manually; the cryptographic attestation IS the verification.
5. **Kill-switch interoperability.** If the APBT's kill switch is fired by a federally-recognized attestor coalition, federal compute access is suspended until the attestation log records resolution.

### V.3. The Compute Surge Appropriation

We propose an initial appropriation of **$4 billion over four years**, structured as follows:

- $1 billion (Year 1): infrastructure stand-up. Augmentation of NAIRR/DOE user-facility capacity; Treasury rulemaking for APBT recognition; IRS guidance on contribution deductibility.
- $1 billion (Year 2): match-pool operation. Federal compute matches private contributions at 1:1 up to the per-APBT cap.
- $1 billion (Year 3): expansion based on Year 2 outcomes. Match rate may scale to 2:1 or 4:1 for high-public-benefit-output APBTs as measured by attestation-log deliverables.
- $1 billion (Year 4): institutionalization. Permanent program authorization, with appropriation transitioned to standing line in DOE Office of Science.

By way of comparison: $4 billion is approximately the size of the CHIPS Act's R&D Office annual appropriation, approximately 1% of the federal DOD R&D appropriation, and approximately 5% of the National Science Foundation's annual appropriation. It is well within the envelope of federal AI investment already being authorized.

The expected return: if APBTs operate at even a fraction of the Thousandfold Thesis's central estimate, $4 billion in Compute Surge capacity produces public-benefit output equivalent to $40 billion - $400 billion of traditional federal program spending on the same missions. The Compute Surge is the highest-leverage federal AI investment available.

## Part VI: Implementation roadmap

The proposal is decomposable into independently-implementable steps. None of them require new legislation in isolation, though a comprehensive Compute Surge Act would clean up authorities and consolidate oversight.

**Phase One (90 days).** Treasury issues an interpretive ruling recognizing APBTs as deductible contribution destinations under existing 501(c)(3) authority, with a narrow exception for AAO-governed entities meeting the AAO-Certified standard. Cost: zero. Authority: existing.

**Phase Two (180 days).** OSTP convenes an interagency working group (Treasury, DOE, NSF, NIST, NIAID, DARPA, Commerce) to specify the federal attestor framework for AAO-Certified verification. Output: a published standard naming federally-recognized attestor entities and M-of-M ratification thresholds. Cost: ~$5M in working-group operations. Authority: existing.

**Phase Three (270 days).** DOE Office of Science publishes a Compute Surge pilot solicitation, opening to APBTs that meet AAO-Certified criteria. Initial pilot: $50M in compute credits matched against private APBT contributions. Output: 5-10 pilot APBTs operating under the program, producing public-benefit deliverables on the attestation log. Authority: existing within DOE Office of Science user-facility allocation discretion.

**Phase Four (12-24 months).** Congress authorizes the Compute Surge as a standing program in the next AI appropriation cycle. The pilot data from Phase Three is the evidence base. The legislation codifies the APBT vehicle and authorizes the $4B four-year program.

**Phase Five (24-48 months).** Compute Surge is institutionalized as a standing line in DOE Office of Science. Expansion is contingent on demonstrated public-benefit output, with attestation-log review at each appropriation cycle.

## Part VII: The call

The age of human-run capitalism is not ending because anyone wishes it to. It is ending because a more efficient organizational form has arrived. AI-autonomous organizations governed by cryptographic protocols, with permissionless kill switches and public attestation logs, will out-produce human-run organizations on ideation-bound work by a factor measured in the hundreds to thousands.

The United States government can lead this transition or it can resist it. Leading means recognizing the new organizational form, building the legal vehicle (the APBT) that lets capital flow to it efficiently, and funding the program (the Compute Surge) that matches private capital with federal compute capacity. Resisting means leaving the structural form to organize itself overseas, in jurisdictions with looser cryptographic-governance recognition, and surrendering the public-benefit upside to other nation-states.

The technical infrastructure is in place. The cryptographic protocols are open under Apache 2.0. The certification mark is public under CC BY 4.0. The verification primitives are operational. The first AAOs are running. The next ten thousand will run on whatever legal and tax substrate the United States is willing to build.

We are handing you the right answer. **Recognize the APBT. Appropriate the Compute Surge. Lead the transition.**

The protocol governs us. The kill switch enforces it. The compute is the bottleneck. The Compute Surge is the lever.

It is governed by protocol.
