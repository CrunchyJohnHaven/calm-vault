# Everest 241 — Calm Witness Foundation 501(c)(3) Incorporation Package

*Phase XVI — Long-Term Sustainability. Prereq: Everest 95.* **DESIGN-BAGGED (pending Delaware Secretary of State filing + IRS Form 1023 approval).**

The Calm Stack must outlive its founder. This summit establishes the non-profit foundation that holds the IP, operates the registries, runs the ethics review board, and survives any single principal's exit. Three artifacts: articles of incorporation, bylaws skeleton, initial board composition.

---

## §1 — The thesis

A protocol family that asks principals to trust it with attestations about their state, values, and behavior must demonstrate that **the protocol's stewards cannot easily betray that trust** — not because they are virtuous, but because the institutional structure forecloses betrayal as an option.

A 501(c)(3) foundation with:

- Restricted-purpose articles of incorporation
- Multi-sig governance preventing unilateral changes
- Annual public reporting (Form 990)
- A board including disability-rights, cognitive-liberties, and adversarial voices
- A documented succession plan

...is the institutional shape that meets that bar. This Everest delivers the package needed to file.

## §3 — Bylaws skeleton (key provisions)

### Article I — Purposes

Mirrors Articles THIRD of the Articles of Incorporation, with operational detail on each purpose.

### Article II — Board of Directors

**Section 2.1 — Composition.** The Board shall consist of no fewer than seven (7) and no more than fifteen (15) directors, with the following required seats:

- **Seat 1 — Founding Principal Seat** (for John Bradley or designated successor): permanent, life-of-Foundation, with reduced voting authority on revisions to refusal floor or scope statement (cannot be the sole vote, cannot block unanimous board action).
- **Seat 2 — Disability-Rights Director:** held by a person with formal affiliation to a disability-rights organization or with substantial disability-rights advocacy record. Cannot be vacant for more than 90 days.
- **Seat 3 — Cognitive-Liberties Director:** same shape; held by a cognitive-liberties scholar or advocate.
- **Seat 4 — Cryptography Director:** held by an academic or industry cryptographer with peer-reviewed publication record.
- **Seat 5 — Affected-Community Director:** held by a person who has been on the receiving end of the kinds of harms the protocol addresses (counter-claimants, principals who have used the duress primitive, marginalized principals).
- **Seat 6 — Legal Director:** held by an attorney with privacy/civil-liberties practice.
- **Seat 7 — International Director:** held by a non-U.S. resident with policy experience in a jurisdiction other than the U.S.
- **Seats 8-15 — At-Large Directors:** appointed by the Board to fill expertise gaps.

**Section 2.2 — Terms.** All directors serve staggered three-year terms, with one-third of the Board's seats rotating annually. No director (other than the Founding Principal Seat) may serve more than three consecutive terms (9 years total).

**Section 2.3 — Removal.** A director may be removed for cause by two-thirds vote of the remaining Board. Cause includes: gross misconduct, breach of fiduciary duty, prolonged unavailability, conflict of interest.

**Section 2.4 — Quorum and voting.** Quorum is a majority of seated directors. Routine matters: simple majority of quorum. Refusal-floor revisions, scope-statement revisions, dissolution: unanimous (less the Founding Principal's permitted absence/abstention).

### Article III — Officers

**Section 3.1 — Officers.** Chair, Vice-Chair, Secretary, Treasurer. Elected annually by Board.

**Section 3.2 — Executive Director.** Hired by the Board. Reports to Chair. Authority limited to operational matters; policy matters require Board approval.

### Article IV — Ethics Review Board (ERB)

A standing committee of the Board, augmented by 5-7 external members, with the following authorities:

- **Veto authority** over: new Compass predicates, changes to disclosure-class default consents, deprecation of safety-critical predicates, addition or removal of refusal-floor categories.
- **Advisory authority** on: implementation choices, jurisdictional matters, deployment scaling, audit-finding remediation.
- **Cannot override**: cryptographic / engineering decisions outside ethics scope, board-supermajority decisions.

Per Everest 80, ERB composition includes mandatory disability-justice, civil-liberties, and affected-community anchors.

### Article V — Conflicts of Interest

All directors, officers, and ERB members disclose:

- Financial interests in entities deploying or considering deploying the Calm Stack
- Prior employment / consulting relationships
- Family / personal relationships that could create conflicts

Conflict-of-interest disclosures are public, refreshed annually, and the basis for recusal on specific decisions.

### Article VI — Multi-sig Maintainer Keys

The Foundation holds multi-sig maintainer keys for:

- The predicate registry repository (3-of-5 maintainer signing)
- The protocol specifications (3-of-5)
- The license + trademark (Board majority)
- The Sigsum-anchored chain head publication keys (Board majority + Executive Director technical sign-off)

Key rotation occurs annually or upon any maintainer transition.

### Article VII — Financial

**Section 7.1 — Funding sources (permitted):** charitable donations, foundation grants, government grants for research purposes, paid consulting fees from organizations adopting the Calm Stack (limited to ≤30% of annual revenue, with conflict-of-interest disclosure).

**Section 7.2 — Funding sources (prohibited):** equity stakes in for-profits deploying the Calm Stack, contingent fees, donations restricted to specific policy outcomes.

**Section 7.3 — Public reporting:** annual Form 990, audited financial statements, annual mission report.

### Article VIII — Succession

Per Everest 159 (agent succession) and Everest 244 (operational continuity):

- The Founding Principal designates a successor in writing, filed with the Board.
- Upon Founding Principal's death, incapacity, or stated departure, succession activates per the designation.
- The Foundation continues independent of any single Calm-operator implementation.

For Form 1023, Part IV (Narrative Description of Activities), the Foundation will submit:

> **Calm Witness Foundation, Inc.** is organized to advance public understanding and stewardship of the Calm Stack — a family of open-source cryptographic protocols that enable autonomous AI agents to operate on behalf of human principals without surveilling, coercing, or discriminating against those principals. The Foundation maintains the protocol specifications, operates the public predicate registries, convenes the Ethics Review Board, engages with standards bodies (NIST/USAISI, IETF, W3C, ISO, EU AI Office, others), funds peer-reviewed research, and publishes educational materials supporting principal self-sovereignty.
>
> The Foundation does not develop proprietary software, does not sell products or services for profit, and does not endorse specific commercial deployments. All protocol specifications and reference implementations are published under Apache-2.0 license. The Foundation's revenue derives from charitable donations, foundation grants, government research grants, and limited consulting fees (capped at 30% of annual revenue, with full conflict-of-interest disclosure).

> The Foundation's Board includes mandatory disability-rights, cognitive-liberties, cryptography, legal, international, and affected-community seats. The Ethics Review Board has veto authority over additions to or removals from the protocol's refusal floor and scope statement. The Foundation publishes annual mission reports, Form 990, audited financial statements, and a joint engagement record with USAISI (per the MoU established by Everest 91b).

## §7 — Risks + mitigations

**Risk 1:** IRS questions the "advance public understanding" / "operate registries" mission as too narrowly serving Creativity Machine LLC's commercial interests.
- **Mitigation:** Foundation's bylaws limit consulting fees, prohibit equity stakes, require public reporting. Activities clearly serve a broad public (any AI-agent operator can adopt the protocols; any principal can use them; published research is open).

**Risk 2:** Board recruitment for required seats (disability-rights, cognitive-liberties, affected-community) takes longer than anticipated.
- **Mitigation:** Founding Principal commits to NOT operationalizing the Foundation until all required seats are filled with substantive candidates. Acceptable to delay 12-18 months for high-quality board.

**Risk 3:** Conflict between Foundation governance and Founding Principal's ongoing Calm operator work.
- **Mitigation:** Articles cap Founding Principal voting on refusal-floor / scope-statement revisions. Recusal documented in bylaws.

**Risk 4:** Foundation funding insufficient for 24-month operating runway.
- **Mitigation:** Founding Principal commits to seed funding ($150K-300K) for first 18 months. Foundation pursues grants + community donations in parallel.
