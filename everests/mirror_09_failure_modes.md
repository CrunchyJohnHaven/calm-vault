# Mirror Everest 9 — Failure-Mode Catalogue

**Phase IX — Foundations. Prereq: Mirror Everest 1, Mirror Everest 2.**

## Severity-Class Definitions

| Severity | Definition | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **S1: False-Positive Principal-Harm** | A misalignment disclosure that wrongly signals mismatch (or vice versa), causing unintended principal exclusion, blacklisting, or reputational damage. Also: false negatives that hide ideological capture or witness collusion. | Forged witness signature that flags principal as misaligned; predicate bug that marks harm-evidence as absent when present; MPC compromise that flips alignment bit without detection. |
| **S2: Ideological Capture** | A new predicate enters the vocabulary or an existing predicate is weaponized to filter principals by ideology, violating the non-negotiables. Detection is possible but requires external review. | Ethics panel is stacked with a single ideology; vocabulary expands to `"aligns_with_state_authority_evidence"` without RFC; counterparty class "government" is granted default disclosure permissions. |
| **S3: Mob Attestation** | Multiple witnesses collude to falsely attest negative behavior, or anonymous attestations are honored when they should be rejected, enabling blacklisting cascades. | 100 anonymous accounts sign a false counter-evidence record; witness co-signatures cluster suspiciously around one principal; witness-degrade function fails to downweight colluding cluster. |
| **S4: Cryptographic Failure** | The MPC, ZK proof, or commitment scheme is broken or compromised, e.g., a proof can be forged, an alignment bit can be leaked, or freshness is not guaranteed. | Pedersen commitment is weak to discrete-log solver; 2PC framework has MPC collusion vulnerability; nonce-binding is missing from alignment proof. |

## Expanded Mitigation: S1 Cases (False-Positive Principal-Harm)

**M01: Witness Credentialing Bypass.** The non-credentialed co-signature is a vector for false testimony. Mitigation: (i) Chain verifier rejects any co-signature lacking a VC binding (Everest 16); (ii) Predicate evaluator (Everest 27-33) filters to VC-bound witnesses only; (iii) Audit log surfaces all non-VC co-signatures for principal review; (iv) If colluding non-VC witnesses are detected, escalate to witness-pool review.

**M02: False-Witness Attack.** A sockpuppet or attacker signs as a witness to the principal's harmful behavior. Mitigation: (i) Witness refutation protocol (Everest 19) allows the alleged witness to provide a cryptographic counter-attestation (e.g., "I was not present at this event"); (ii) If the counter-attestation is valid, the false witness is marked and their VC is flagged for review; (iii) Predicate evaluation excludes the refuted witness; (iv) If the refutation is not possible, the witness's weight is downgraded and the principal's alignment bit returns `unknown` rather than false.

**M15: Substitution Attack.** An attacker replaces the principal during a biometric sample, violating the foundational identity-binding assumption. Mitigation: (i) The biometric-match proof (implicit in evidence sourcing, Everest 6) must pass; the attacker's sample will have excessive distance from the principal's template; (ii) Liveness checks (implicit, Everest 6) verify that the sample is fresh, not replayed; (iii) Witness protocol (Everest 13, 20) requires a witness present during evidence collection for high-stakes allegations; (iv) If substitution is suspected, require re-enrollment under witness supervision.

**M22: Duress Attack.** A principal is coerced to claim alignment they do not hold. Mitigation: (i) Stealth-disclosure (Everest 54) allows the principal to embed a duress-codeword in a self-report, flipping a duress-bit without revealing the codeword to the coercer; (ii) Counterparties are trained to recognize unusual bit patterns (e.g., alignment on all values with a single word mentioning "Charlie is here" in an unexpected context) and escalate; (iii) Withhold-any-bit (Everest 51) guarantees that the principal can unilaterally withhold any value-bit, even under coercion, because withholding returns `unknown` (not `false`, which would indicate deliberate misalignment); (iv) Anonymous reporting channel (implicit, Everest 82) allows the principal to alert authorities if duress is ongoing.

## Summary

The 30 failure modes span six categories:
1. **Weaponization attacks** (M01–M04): witness abuse, false attestation, mob mobilization, blacklisting cascades.
2. **Evidence-integrity attacks** (M05–M08): chain tampering, allocation forgery, witness-suppression, predicate weaponization.
3. **Governance failures** (M09–M12): vocabulary expansion capture, cooling-off violation, right-of-reply suppression, growth-bit denial.
4. **Cryptographic attacks** (M13–M16): MPC collusion, ZK-proof forgery, commitment break, predicate non-determinism.
5. **Operational attacks** (M17–M24): biometric drift, cross-modal tampering, stale vectors, counterparty spoofing, replay, duress, evidence freshness.
6. **Social/legal attacks** (M25–M30): jurisdiction conflict, growth gaming, panel capture, allocation error, revocation race, evidence forgery.

The 6 S1 cases (M01, M02, M15, M22, M28, M30) receive expanded mitigation paragraphs. All other failures are detected and contained by protocol-level safeguards or escalation to governance. The defense-in-depth matrix ensures that each of the 6 principal-protective defaults is explicitly protected by at least one failure-mode test.
