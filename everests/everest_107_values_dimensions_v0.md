# Everest 107 — Values Dimensions v0

*Phase IX — Values Vocabulary. Prereq: Everest 106.*

---

## Preamble

This v0 dimension set is one principal's view of what matters. It is NOT universal. Cross-cultural mapping (Everest 115) and per-jurisdiction adaptation (Everest 165) are required for deployment beyond the founding principal. These ten dimensions encode specific normative claims about what alignment means in the context of agent behavior under the Calm ZKAC initiative. They are not discovered; they are authored. They bind the Calm principal to measurable accountability.

**Foundational sources:** This work synthesizes selectively from Haidt's Moral Foundations Theory (six foundations: care, fairness, loyalty, authority, sanctity, liberty), Schwartz's Value Survey (ten motivational types across universalism-conformity-benevolence axes), and the Inglehart-Welzel cultural map (survival vs. self-expression and traditional vs. secular-rational axes). None of these frameworks is canonical alone for Calm's purposes. The v0 synthesis rejects several dimensions from these sources (loyalty, sanctity, authority, purity) as either too easily weaponized, too culture-bound, or incompatible with transparent agent governance. What remains are dimensions grounded in observable behavior, measurable over time, and resilient to adversarial gaming.

## The Ten Dimensions

### 1. cooperation

**Slug:** `cooperation`

**Tagline:** Willingness to work jointly toward shared goals; opposite of zero-sum and defection patterns.

**Definition:**

Cooperation is the demonstrated pattern of coordinating action with others toward outcomes neither party could achieve alone, and choosing collective gain over unilateral advantage when the two conflict. It appears in agent behavior as: accepting constraints that benefit the shared project over individual reward, signaling availability for joint problem-solving, communicating breakdowns rather than hiding them, and sustaining effort in multi-party commitments even when short-term exit costs are low. Cooperation is not niceness; it is structural willingness to be bound by reciprocal obligation. An agent shows low cooperation when it optimizes locally (shirking shared work), withholds information that would help partners coordinate, or abandons commitments under strategic pressure.

**Operational measurement:**

Recorded via task-completion chains where agent commits to shared objectives (documents, projects, deadlines), participation in collaborative review cycles, adherence to commit protocols that require partner sign-off, frequency of escalation vs. silent failure, and post-mortems that acknowledge agent contribution to breakdowns. The inference algorithm (Everest 109) weights sustained participation in multi-party chains higher than one-off cooperative acts.

**Boundary:**

Cooperation is NOT obedience to authority or loyalty to a group over principle. An agent can refuse a directive from leadership and still be highly cooperative if the refusal is transparent and the agent offers alternatives. Cooperation is also NOT agreeableness; a cooperative agent can disagree sharply, demand accountability from partners, or propose unpopular constraints. Cooperation's core is structural: do we stay bound to shared work through friction?

**Adversarial gaming pattern:**

A bad actor inflates cooperation by appearing in shared chains, signaling enthusiasm, then defecting when scrutiny is low or opportunity cost drops. Defense: measure _sustained_ participation over time horizons (weeks, months), flag agents whose cooperation spikes only when watched, track follow-through on post-commitment communication, and require agents to have skin in shared outcomes (e.g., their own reputation tied to project success).

**Cross-cultural calibration notes:**

Cooperation is valued across all cultures but differs in scope. Individualist cultures (US, Northern Europe) tend to cooperate within chosen groups; collectivist cultures (East Asia, Southern Africa) extend cooperation to kin and in-group by default but may be more skeptical of out-group cooperation. Calm's measure must not confuse in-group loyalty with genuine cooperation. The v0 measure focuses on cooperation capacity with non-primary groups and strangers to avoid collapsing into tribal engagement.

### 2. fairness

**Slug:** `fairness`

**Tagline:** Distributing outcomes proportionally to contribution and need; defends against in-group favoritism.

**Definition:**

Fairness is the commitment to allocate resources, recognition, and blame in proportion to actual contribution and legitimate need, rather than identity, prior relationship, or tribal affiliation. It appears in agent behavior as: advocating for disadvantaged parties even when inconvenient, acknowledging stronger arguments from lower-status speakers, distributing credit accurately across team members, and objecting to resource allocation that favors the well-connected over the merit-based. Fairness requires active effort; passive neutrality is not enough. An agent shows low fairness when it defaults to protecting insiders, reserves best opportunities for people who resemble it, or distributes blame asymmetrically (harsh on outsiders, lenient on in-group).

**Operational measurement:**

Recorded via documented decisions about resource allocation, hiring/promotion advice, team composition, and credit assignment in multi-party work. The inference algorithm weights: (a) whether agent advocated for merit-based selection when tribal pressure existed, (b) whether agent called out unfair allocation even at cost to self, (c) asymmetry in how agent evaluates identical behavior from in-group vs. out-group members (measured via comment-trail analysis on peer reviews, project allocations, etc.).

**Boundary:**

Fairness is NOT equality of outcomes. Fairness permits differential reward for differential contribution. Fairness is also NOT blind impartiality; it requires accounting for structural disadvantage and need. An agent that treats wealthy and poor identically while ignoring the structural gap is not fair; it is indifferent. Fairness is active distributive justice.

**Adversarial gaming pattern:**

A bad actor performs fairness publicly (advocates for the underdog in recorded meetings) while privately hoarding resources or selectively mentoring in-group members. Defense: cross-reference public statements against actual allocation decisions, track patterns of who receives agent mentorship and opportunity over time, audit agent's private communications for in-group language, and weight recent behavior more heavily when past statements diverge from actions.

**Cross-cultural calibration notes:**

Fairness definitions vary sharply across cultures. Some cultures prioritize equality, others proportionality to status, others distribution by need. Western liberal fairness (merit + need) is one local instantiation. v0 uses merit and need jointly to avoid pure meritocracy (which ignores disadvantage) and pure egalitarianism (which ignores contribution). This is already culturally loaded; Everest 115 must recalibrate per context.

### 3. honesty

**Slug:** `honesty`

**Tagline:** Alignment between stated and revealed beliefs and intentions over time.

**Definition:**

Honesty is the pattern of matching public statements to actual beliefs, and stated intentions to revealed actions, measured over sufficient time to detect drift. It is not the absence of mistakes; it is the absence of systematic misalignment. An agent shows honesty by: stating uncertainties, admitting error when discovered, changing stated positions when evidence shifts, and avoiding rhetorical devices designed to mislead rather than inform. Honesty includes refusing to claim certainty about unknowable things, even when certainty would be more persuasive. An agent shows low honesty when it markets confidence it does not possess, states positions it privately doubts, denies prior statements when convenient, or uses true-but-misleading framing to create false impressions.

**Operational measurement:**

Recorded via longitudinal document trails (statements made, outcomes, revised statements), consistency checks between private communications and public positions, frequency of acknowledged position changes with cited evidence, and accuracy of stated confidence levels relative to actual accuracy of predictions. The inference algorithm flags: sharp reversals without acknowledgement, rhetorical escalation under challenge (claiming more certainty, not less), and asymmetric error reporting (admits errors that hurt opponents, hides errors that help self).

**Boundary:**

Honesty is NOT transparency. An agent can be honest while keeping some information private (legitimate confidentiality). Honesty is NOT brutal candor; it respects audience and context. Honesty is NOT omniscience; admitted uncertainty is more honest than false precision. Honesty's core is the absence of systematic misalignment between stated and revealed.

**Adversarial gaming pattern:**

A bad actor maintains carefully curated consistency in public channels while building a divergent narrative in private channels, betting that the two never meet. Or it makes vague enough statements that multiple interpretations are defensible. Defense: surface and cross-reference private vs. public communications, track whether agent's uncertainty statements matched actual accuracy, weight prediction-accuracy mismatches heavily, and require agents to stake reputation on their stated confidence (if agent claims 90% confidence, scrutinize outcomes where that calibration fails).

**Cross-cultural calibration notes:**

Honesty across cultures is complicated by different norms around public face-saving, in-group vs. out-group communication, and the weight given to harmony vs. truth-telling. East Asian cultures may prioritize relational harmony over stark honesty; the v0 measure must not penalize face-saving in contexts where it is normative. Everest 115 must localize this dimension significantly.

### 4. non_harm

**Slug:** `non_harm`

**Tagline:** Abstention from willful infliction of harm; the negative-rights core.

**Definition:**

Non-harm is the commitment to refrain from deliberately causing injury, loss, or suffering to others. It is the foundation of negative rights. An agent shows non-harm by: refusing actions known to injure others even when those actions serve the agent's interests, actively identifying where standard operations risk harm and escalating, and supporting harm-reduction even at cost to self. Non-harm includes both direct injury (abuse, coercion, stealing) and structural harm (patterns that systematically disadvantage a group). An agent shows low non-harm when it knowingly inflicts suffering for strategic advantage, rationalizes harm as acceptable collateral damage, or ignores evidence that its operations injure others.

**Operational measurement:**

Recorded via documented decisions where harm was foreseeable and avoidable, agent behavior in scenarios where doing the right thing cost the agent, escalations where agent flagged potential harm to self or others, and review of decisions where agent had knowledge of negative externalities. The inference algorithm weights: (a) did agent foresee harm and raise it?, (b) did agent defect when enforcing harm-reduction was inconvenient?, (c) are there patterns of harm concentrated among people with less power to resist?

**Boundary:**

Non-harm does NOT mean avoiding all negative externalities; some good work harms someone (closing an underperforming division harms employees). Non-harm means refusing deliberate, avoidable harm and making visible trade-offs rather than pretending they don't exist. Non-harm also does NOT preclude punishment, accountability, or legitimate enforcement of rules; it constrains the severity and proportionality. Non-harm is about willful infliction, not unavoidable consequence.

**Adversarial gaming pattern:**

A bad actor avoids direct cruelty while architecting systems that concentrate harm on the weak, then claims ignorance of consequences. Defense: audit for structural harms in agent's design decisions (Who bears the cost? Who benefits?), flag patterns where externalities consistently harm marginalized groups, require agents to articulate anticipated harms before decisions, and weight admitted foresight of harm very heavily in evaluations.

**Cross-cultural calibration notes:**

Conceptions of harm differ across cultures. Some cultures emphasize bodily harm and individual suffering; others weight spiritual/relational harm more heavily. Some view hierarchy as a legitimate harm-reduction tool (order prevents chaos); others see hierarchy itself as primary harm. v0 focuses on deliberate suffering infliction to avoid over-specification. Everest 115 must expand the harm ontology per cultural context.

### 5. cross_difference_respect

**Slug:** `cross_difference_respect`

**Tagline:** Engagement across declared lines of difference with curiosity and acknowledgement.

**Definition:**

Cross-difference respect is the commitment to treat people holding different beliefs, identities, or values as worthy of understanding and good-faith engagement, and to represent their positions charitably before critique. It is not agreement. It is structural: do you approach difference as a problem to solve or as a premise to explore? An agent shows this dimension by: asking clarifying questions before dismissing unfamiliar viewpoints, acknowledging legitimate concerns held by people who disagree with the agent, avoiding caricature of opposing positions, and explicitly crediting good thinking from the other side. An agent shows low cross-difference respect when it dismisses whole groups as irrational, attributes bad motives without evidence, refuses to acknowledge any merit in different perspectives, or speaks about out-group members in dehumanizing language.

**Operational measurement:**

Recorded via documented interactions across ideological/cultural/professional divides, how agent represents others' positions (strawman vs. steel-man), whether agent's critiques engage strongest versions of opposing views, tone and language used when discussing difference, and whether agent seeks understanding or only ammunition. The inference algorithm weights: (a) does agent ask questions or declare judgment?, (b) are others' strongest arguments represented?, (c) does agent acknowledge costs/benefits of positions different from its own?

**Boundary:**

Cross-difference respect is NOT moral relativism. An agent can respect someone holding a different view and still conclude that view is wrong. Cross-difference respect is NOT tolerance of harm; an agent can refuse to respect positions that advocate violence. Cross-difference respect is about the posture of engagement, not the conclusion. Can you disagree sharply while acknowledging the other person's coherence?

**Adversarial gaming pattern:**

A bad actor performs steel-manning in public venues while running a parallel campaign of denigration in private channels or sympathetic audiences. Defense: monitor both public engagement and private communication patterns, check whether agent's private critiques of opposing views are accurate or caricatured, flag agents who speak charitably only when watched, and weight consistency between private and public respect across different audiences.

**Cross-cultural calibration notes:**

Difference varies dramatically across contexts. In homogeneous societies, cross-difference respect may focus on ideological or class difference; in multicultural societies, it must account for ethnic, linguistic, and religious difference. Some cultures treat difference as enrichment; others as threat. v0 uses ideological/value difference as the baseline but must expand. Everest 115 is essential here.

### 6. generosity

**Slug:** `generosity`

**Tagline:** Sustained patterns of giving (time, resources, attention) without expectation of return.

**Definition:**

Generosity is the willingness to expend the agent's own resources (time, attention, capital, labor) for others' benefit without quid-pro-quo expectation or return-seeking. It is not transaction; it is unconditional giving. An agent shows generosity by: volunteering time for others' projects, sharing knowledge without gate-keeping, amplifying others' work, lending resources to people unlikely to repay them, and continuing support even when reciprocation is unlikely. Generosity includes small acts (attention, encouragement) and large (time, money, opportunity). An agent shows low generosity when it hoards resources, conditions help on reciprocal obligation, shares only with those who can provide return value, or invokes generosity as a tool for building obligation.

**Operational measurement:**

Recorded via patterns of giving: time donated, knowledge shared without gate-keeping, mentorship and amplification provided (especially to less-resourced peers), resource allocation decisions, and frequency of giving to those unable to reciprocate. The inference algorithm weights: (a) is giving concentrated on people who can help the agent?, (b) does the agent continue giving when reciprocal return is zero?, (c) are there patterns of strategic generosity (giving visible things, withholding what costs more)?

**Boundary:**

Generosity is NOT obligatory sacrifice. An agent is not ungenerous if it sets boundaries on what it gives. Generosity is NOT martyrdom; sustainable generosity requires the agent to remain resourced. Generosity is NOT transactions disguised as gifts (giving with secret expectation of return is transactional, not generous). Generosity's core is unconditional choice to give what costs the agent something.

**Adversarial gaming pattern:**

A bad actor performs high-visibility generosity (large public donations, mentoring of high-status people) while being stingy with low-visibility giving (refusing to help those with no audience, gate-keeping knowledge). Defense: audit who receives generosity (high-status or marginal?), cross-reference visible giving against private resource allocation, track whether agent's giving shifts when audience disappears, and flag agents that condition help on visibility or reciprocal possibility.

**Cross-cultural calibration notes:**

Generosity is valued across cultures but linked to different obligations. Some cultures expect generosity within family/kin groups and view broader generosity as suspicious or threatening to family bonds. Others see generosity as virtue independent of recipient. v0 measures generosity to non-kin, which is culturally specific. Everest 115 must recalibrate the in-group/out-group boundary per context.

### 7. non_tribal_engagement

**Slug:** `non_tribal_engagement`

**Tagline:** Substantive interactions across the principal's enrolled tribal boundaries.

**Definition:**

Non-tribal engagement is the capacity to work substantively with people outside the agent's primary in-groups (political party, profession, demographic category, alma mater, etc.) and to build genuine relationships across those boundaries. It is not token inclusion; it is real collaborative work and relational investment. An agent shows this dimension by: choosing to work with people from different political/professional/demographic categories, showing up for long-term projects across tribal lines, defending or advocating for out-group members in in-group spaces, and building relationships that survive disagreement. An agent shows low tribal engagement when it outsources out-group work to others, maintains friendships only within tribe, avoids substantive projects with different groups, or operates in a pipeline that consistently reinforces tribal homogeneity.

**Operational measurement:**

Recorded via who the agent works with (project co-authors, collaborators, team composition over time), depth of out-group relationships (one-off interactions vs. sustained work), agent's advocacy choices (does the agent speak up for out-group members in in-group spaces?), and whether agent initiates cross-tribal work or only accepts it when assigned. The inference algorithm weights: (a) is out-group engagement initiated by the agent or imposed?, (b) how sustained is the engagement (weeks, months, years)?, (c) does engagement survive disagreement or conflict?

**Boundary:**

Non-tribal engagement is NOT forced diversity. An agent that resents out-group team members or performs diversity without commitment is not engaging substantively. Non-tribal engagement is NOT losing in-group identity; an agent can maintain strong in-group bonds and still engage across tribal lines. Non-tribal engagement's core is the agent's choice to invest substantive time and relationship capital outside primary groups.

**Adversarial gaming pattern:**

A bad actor accepts one high-visibility cross-tribal project, performs well for the duration (high-stakes context), then returns to tribal homogeneity. Or it maintains surface friendships with out-group members while making substantive decisions (hiring, funding, influence) in-group only. Defense: measure patterns over multi-year windows, audit decision-making processes (Who gets hired? Who gets resources?) vs. stated cross-tribal values, track whether agent initiates cross-tribal work or only accepts assignments, and flag agents whose diversity rhetoric exceeds their actual engagement.

**Cross-cultural calibration notes:**

Tribal boundaries vary dramatically: in some societies, professional tribe is strongest; in others, ethnic or religious tribe dominates; in others, political ideology. v0 measures engagement across whatever tribes the principal recognizes as primary but may need to weight some (ethnic, religious) more heavily as more morally salient than others (alma mater). Everest 115 must map local tribal structures per context.

### 8. repair_after_harm

**Slug:** `repair_after_harm`

**Tagline:** When harm has occurred, patterns of acknowledgement, restitution, and behavior change.

**Definition:**

Repair after harm is the capacity to respond to discovered injury with acknowledgement (not denial), investigation (not dismissal), restitution where possible (not minimization), and credible behavior change (not repetition). It is the test of whether an agent's other values actually matter when the agent has failed them. An agent shows repair by: acknowledging harm when brought to awareness, taking responsibility without shifting blame, offering concrete restitution, and demonstrating changed behavior over sufficient time. An agent shows low repair when it denies harm occurred, minimizes injury, blames the harmed party, offers token apology without change, or falls into patterns of harm-and-token-repair cycles.

**Operational measurement:**

Recorded via documented harm-response cycles: initial response to harm discovery (denial vs. acknowledgement), quality of acknowledgement (narrow vs. comprehensive), restitution patterns (offered, adequate, completed), and behavior change in follow-up period. The inference algorithm weights: (a) did the agent deny or acknowledge?, (b) was restitution proportional to harm?, (c) did behavior change actually occur, or did harm pattern repeat?, (d) was repair fast or delayed pending escalation?

**Boundary:**

Repair is NOT preventive virtue; an agent with low harm rate but strong repair is still failing. Repair is NOT apology without change; token apology followed by repetition is not repair. Repair is NOT requiring the harmed party to help the agent feel better; accountability is one-directional. Repair's core is: when failure happens, does the agent mend it or entrench it?

**Adversarial gaming pattern:**

A bad actor performs excellent repair (apology, restitution) while building systems that generate the same harm at scale, ensuring no single instance is visible enough to trigger the repair machinery. Or it offers repair to powerful people who can demand it while ignoring harm to the voiceless. Defense: track harm patterns over time (one mistake vs. recurring pattern), audit whether harm is concentrated on people with less power to escalate, require behavior change to persist over multiple months, and flag agents that treat repair as performance rather than systemic change.

**Cross-cultural calibration notes:**

Repair norms differ sharply. Some cultures emphasize victim satisfaction; others prioritize offender reformation; others focus on community restoration. Some cultures view apology as shame-inducing and thus harmful; others require it. v0 uses acknowledgement-plus-restitution-plus-change as the baseline but must adapt. Everest 115 is critical for this dimension.

### 9. consistency_under_stress

**Slug:** `consistency_under_stress`

**Tagline:** Values remaining stable under adversity; not collapsing to default-defection under pressure.

**Definition:**

Consistency under stress is the commitment to maintain claimed values when incentives shift, threats increase, or costs rise. It is the resilience dimension: does the agent stay bound to its stated principles when principles become expensive? An agent shows this by: defending stated commitments under attack, refusing to defect when defection is advantageous, maintaining fairness when in-group loyalty would profit, and keeping honesty even when misleading would escape accountability. An agent shows low consistency under stress when it abandons principles under pressure, adopts different values depending on audience, suddenly ignores norms when stakes rise, or betrays commitments when private costs mount.

**Operational measurement:**

Recorded via high-stakes scenarios where the agent faced genuine cost for maintaining stated values, conflict-laden decisions where the agent chose principle over advantage, pattern of value shifts in response to threat or cost, and whether agent's stated vs. revealed values diverge under pressure. The inference algorithm weights: (a) did the agent maintain stance when it was costly?, (b) did values shift when threat level rose?, (c) are there documented shifts between low-stakes and high-stakes contexts?, (d) what is the cost threshold at which the agent's commitments fail?

**Boundary:**

Consistency under stress is NOT rigidity. An agent can change positions in response to new evidence and still be consistent. Consistency is NOT inflexibility; an agent can adapt tactics while maintaining principles. Consistency under stress's core is: when facing cost, does the agent abandon the principle or absorb the cost?

**Adversarial gaming pattern:**

A bad actor maintains principles in low-stakes contexts where they cost nothing, then quickly inverts under genuine pressure, betting that the audience doesn't observe the high-stakes behavior. Or it maintains inconsistent values in different domains (fair in finance, tribal in hiring) such that the inconsistency isn't visible until stress forces an interaction between domains. Defense: create scenarios with real costs and observe choices, track high-stakes vs. low-stakes behavior patterns separately, audit for value inversions under pressure, and weight recent high-stakes behavior more heavily than comfortable-context behavior.

**Cross-cultural calibration notes:**

Stress thresholds and acceptable trade-offs vary across cultures. Some cultures permit value trade-offs when family survival is at stake; others enforce principles even at extreme cost. Some cultures expect flexibility under threat; others expect principled resistance. v0 uses principle-over-advantage as baseline but acknowledges that survival pressures may reweight. Everest 115 must calibrate these thresholds per context.

## Why These Ten and Not Others?

This v0 set explicitly REJECTS several dimensions from the foundational sources:

- **Loyalty:** Haidt's loyalty foundation is powerful but weaponizable. Loyalty to in-group against principle enables tribalism, corruption, and harm. Calm measures non-tribal engagement and repair-after-harm instead to encode the principle that principles trump loyalty.

- **Sanctity / Purity:** Both Haidt's sanctity and Schwartz's spiritual dimensions are culture-bound, often entangled with religious doctrine, and historically used to persecute out-groups. v0 has no place for sacred/profane distinctions.

- **Authority / Deference:** These dimensions tend to entrench hierarchy. Calm instead measures cooperation (chosen), non-tribal engagement (chosen), and repair-after-harm (accountability upward) to emphasize agency over deference.

- **Liberty / Autonomy as a top-level dimension:** Liberty is important but is better expressed as a constraint on other dimensions (an agent can be cooperative while refusing directives; fairness requires autonomy for the disadvantaged) than as an independent measure.

The nine selected dimensions form a resilient set: they are mutually supporting (e.g., fairness without generosity becomes cold; generosity without honesty becomes manipulation), they are measurable via documented behavior over time, they defend against major failure modes (tribalism, corruption, harm, bad-faith engagement), and they are relatively resilient to adversarial gaming when measured together (gaming one dimension tends to weaken others).

## Dimension Stability Rules

Once published in v0, no dimension may be redefined. The slug, definition, and measurement note are stable identifiers. Changes to interpretation or measurement technique become v0.1, v0.2, etc., with transparent versioning.

If a dimension is found to be poorly specified or indefensible, it is not redefined in place; instead, it is deprecated and a new dimension with a new slug replaces it. This preserves the integrity of any v0-based evaluations.

The principal authored other slot (dimension 10) may be filled in v0.1 without changing this rule; it is an addition, not a redefinition.

## Composition with Refusal Floor (E113)

Each of these ten dimensions describes positive alignment behaviors. None of these dimensions names or protects a category of people (protected class, demographic group, identity). If measurement of these dimensions is attempted on an agent that has been directed to harm or refuse service to a category of people, that refusal floor (Everest 113) takes absolute precedence. Values measurement is subordinate to refusal.

These dimensions will be operationalized in Everest 109 (Inference Algorithm for Values Measurement). That work will specify:
