# Everest 115 — Cross-Cultural Values Mapping

*Phase IX — Values Vocabulary. Prereq: Everest 107, 20.*

---

## Preamble

The ten dimensions of values established in Everest 107 (non-harm, repair-after-harm, consistency-under-stress, cooperation, fairness, honesty, cross-difference-respect, generosity, non-tribal-engagement, and principal-authored-other) emerged from a research lineage shaped by Western secular individualism, Enlightenment philosophy, and contemporary organizational psychology. Deploying these dimensions as universal—without acknowledgment of their cultural origins and without calibration mechanisms—would constitute a form of cultural imperialism: treating one culture's wisdom as a tribunal for all others.

This document accepts that variation: it maps where the dimensions remain robust across cultures, where operationalizations differ significantly, and how the Calm protocol can honor both consistency and cultural context. The goal is not to relativize harm or to assume convergence, but to be honest about what the protocol is: a tool built in a particular tradition, now extended globally with accountability.

---

## Part 1: Universal Dimensions (Robust Across Cultures)

Four dimensions show robust cross-cultural measurement and similar operationalization:

### Non-Harm
Abstention from willful harm—the avoidance of violence, torture, deprivation, and coercion—is documented across all cultures studied by anthropology and cross-cultural psychology (Henrich, 2020). Cultural variation exists in *scope* (whom is protected) and *degrees* (what counts as harm), but the principle itself is not culturally relative. Even cultures with high tolerance for conflict recognize a distinction between harm and competition.

**Implication:** Non-harm thresholds remain consistent across all cultural calibrations.

### Repair-After-Harm
Every culture studied has institutions for repairing harm: compensation, restitution, confession, reconciliation, ritual cleansing. The *form* varies (monetary, ritual, relational, legal) and the *speed* differs, but the pattern is universal. Cultures that prize honor may demand public acknowledgment; cultures that prize harmony may require private restoration; but both categories engage repair.

**Implication:** The repair dimension is calibrated for cultural preference in *mechanism*, not *principle*.

### Consistency-Under-Stress
The ability to sustain values when under duress, resource scarcity, fear, or grief is universally measurable. Some cultures explicitly train this (Stoicism, Buddhist practice, martial codes); others develop it through hardship. No culture celebrates value-abandonment under stress; cultural variation exists in *which values* are prioritized when all cannot be honored, not in the measurement of consistency itself.

**Implication:** Consistency thresholds are culturally invariant, though the *ranking* of values under conflict varies.

### Principal-Authored-Other
By design, this dimension requires principals to articulate their own values in their own context. Cultural variation is the *point*. No calibration is needed; the dimension itself is a placeholder for cultural specificity.

**Implication:** This dimension functions as a check on universalism; no adjustment required.

## Part 2: Culturally-Variable Dimensions

Six dimensions require explicit operationalization by cultural context:

### Cooperation
**Western individualist framing:** Cooperation = voluntary participation in groups that serve individual or collective goals; preference for teams, markets, and formal organizations.

**Collectivist framing:** Cooperation = maintenance of kin-network and in-group obligations; measured by adherence to relational duties (filial piety, clan loyalty, reciprocal obligation within defined circles).

**Cultural clusters and adjustments:**
- **English-speaking secular individualist (+0%):** Cooperation scored on breadth (willingness to join diverse groups) and flexibility (ability to shift group membership).
- **Confucian heritage (+15%):** Cooperation scored on depth (commitment to hierarchical roles and family-first duty); voluntary cooperation outside prescribed roles scores lower.
- **Latin Catholic (+10%):** Cooperation scored on community participation and church/neighborhood solidarity; but with flexibility in scope.
- **Islamic/Middle Eastern (+12%):** Cooperation scored on umma (community of faith) and kin-network loyalty; formal secular organizations score lower unless religiously aligned.
- **African collectivist (+18%):** Cooperation scored on Ubuntu-style interdependence; measured by willingness to subordinate individual interest to community welfare; emphasis on relational capital.

**Baseline threshold adjustment:** -0.15 to +0.18 depending on context.

### Fairness
**Western framing:** Fairness = proportionality (reward for contribution); equality before rules; transparent, rule-based systems; merit-based allocation.

**Non-Western framings:** Fairness = need-based redistribution (those with greater needs receive more); equity (different treatment for different situations); relational repair (fairness restores harmony, not merely rights).

**Cultural clusters and adjustments:**
- **English-speaking secular individualist (+0%):** Fairness measured as rule consistency and meritocratic outcome.
- **Confucian heritage (+12%):** Fairness includes hierarchical appropriateness (a ruler treats subjects differently than peers); fairness = each receives role-appropriate benefit.
- **Latin Catholic (+8%):** Fairness emphasizes need-based redistribution and communal solidarity; a leader is fair if the poor are cared for.
- **Islamic/Middle Eastern (+10%):** Fairness = justice tempered by mercy; zakat (almsgiving) and community care are fairness obligations, not charity.
- **African collectivist (+14%):** Fairness = restorative and redistributive; measured by whether community wholeness is maintained, not individual rights vindication.

**Baseline threshold adjustment:** -0.12 to +0.14 depending on context.

### Honesty
**Western framing:** Honesty = factual accuracy; statements map to external reality; deception = moral failure.

**Face-saving cultural framing:** Honesty = preserving dignity and social harmony; a statement that damages reputation (even if factually true) is dishonest; social truth (what allows relationships to flourish) can supersede factual truth.

**Cultural clusters and adjustments:**
- **English-speaking secular individualist (+0%):** Honesty scored as factual accuracy and explicit transparency.
- **Confucian heritage (+18%):** Honesty includes social appropriateness; public contradiction of authority (even if factually correct) scores lower; discretion in sensitive matters is valued.
- **Latin Catholic (+8%):** Honesty prioritizes truth-telling, but mercy and pastoral care can justify withholding full facts (confessional seal, for example).
- **Islamic/Middle Eastern (+15%):** Honesty includes taqiyyah (permissible concealment to protect faith); honesty to fellow believers is stricter than to those outside; protection of family honor justifies discretion.
- **African collectivist (+12%):** Honesty includes relational appropriateness; public shaming (even if factually grounded) is dishonest; truth-telling is calibrated to relationship.

**Baseline threshold adjustment:** -0.18 to +0.18 depending on context.

### Cross-Difference Respect
**Definition:** The capacity to engage, learn from, and accord dignity to those whose values, beliefs, or practices differ from one's own.

**Challenge:** What counts as "difference" varies. In the West, religion and ideology are salient differences. In caste-based societies, caste is salient. In segmented societies, ethnicity is salient. Respect for difference in *one* dimension does not imply respect across *all* dimensions.

**Cultural clusters and adjustments:**
- **English-speaking secular individualist (+0%):** Cross-difference respect measured across ideology, lifestyle, and belief systems; religious difference is prominent.
- **Confucian heritage (+12%):** Cross-difference respect includes generational and hierarchical difference; respecting elders and authority is part of this dimension; ideological difference is less salient (more emphasis on unity).
- **Latin Catholic (+10%):** Cross-difference respect includes religious difference and class difference; Catholic social teaching emphasizes dignity of the poor and marginalized.
- **Islamic/Middle Eastern (+14%):** Cross-difference respect for People of the Book (other Abrahamic faiths) is explicit; sectarian and political difference can be more contested.
- **African collectivist (+16%):** Cross-difference respect includes ethnic and linguistic diversity within a shared communal commitment; measured by willingness to engage across clan or ethnic lines while maintaining community ties.

**Baseline threshold adjustment:** -0.12 to +0.16 depending on context.

### Generosity
**Western framing:** Generosity = voluntary transfer of resources (money, time, attention) to those outside one's immediate circle; motivated by choice and altruism.

**Communal framing:** Generosity = fulfillment of obligatory sharing norms; Ubuntu ("I am because we are") implies that sharing is not choice but relational duty; gift economies function differently from market exchange.

**Cultural clusters and adjustments:**
- **English-speaking secular individualist (+0%):** Generosity measured as discretionary giving; gifts to strangers and charitable giving score highest.
- **Confucian heritage (+10%):** Generosity includes filial piety and family support; giving to extended kin is expected and scores high; giving to strangers is lower-status.
- **Latin Catholic (+12%):** Generosity measured by care for the poor and vulnerable; communal obligation to support the marginalized.
- **Islamic/Middle Eastern (+14%):** Generosity is zakat (obligatory almsgiving, not charity); waqf (endowment for community); measured by commitment to community welfare.
- **African collectivist (+18%):** Generosity = Ubuntu-style sharing; willingness to redistribute wealth to maintain community balance; sharing is obligatory, not supererogatory; measured by degree of redistribution.

**Baseline threshold adjustment:** -0.10 to +0.18 depending on context.

### Non-Tribal-Engagement
**Definition:** The capacity to collaborate, trust, and contribute outside one's in-group.

**Challenge:** The definition of "tribe" varies. Western liberal individualism treats tribe as kinship, ethnicity, or possibly hometown. Caste-based systems define tribe by caste. Sectarian societies define tribe by religion. Professional communities define tribe by role. A person can be deeply tribal within their definition while appearing cosmopolitan by another culture's definition.

**Cultural clusters and adjustments:**
- **English-speaking secular individualist (+0%):** Non-tribal engagement measured by willingness to work across political affiliation, religion, and socioeconomic background.
- **Confucian heritage (+15%):** Non-tribal engagement includes hierarchy-crossing (working across class and rank); particularism is higher (stronger preference for known groups); engagement outside family/company is lower baseline.
- **Latin Catholic (+8%):** Non-tribal engagement includes class crossing (church as universal community); but particularism within parish/diocese is expected.
- **Islamic/Middle Eastern (+12%):** Non-tribal engagement includes umma (all Muslims); but sectarian lines and tribal lines can be strong; engagement outside faith community is more difficult.
- **African collectivist (+20%):** Non-tribal engagement includes inter-clan and inter-ethnic collaboration; but extended kinship obligations remain primary; cosmopolitanism is measured against deep relational ties.

**Baseline threshold adjustment:** -0.15 to +0.20 depending on context.

## Part 3: The Calibration Mechanism

### Enrollment-Time Declaration
At enrollment, a principal **optionally** declares their primary cultural context. This declaration is:
- **Not mandatory** (principals may decline to specify or may select "mixed/hybrid")
- **Private to the principal** (never shared with counterparties or disclosed in reports)
- **Used only for internal calibration** of threshold-setting and scoring
- **Overridable** (the principal can manually adjust any calibration)

Cultural contexts offered: English-speaking secular individualist, Confucian heritage, Latin Catholic, Islamic/Middle Eastern, African collectivist, and "Other/Hybrid" (with optional annotation for the principal's own reference).

### Threshold Adjustment
Default thresholds for the six variable dimensions shift by the percentages noted above, applied at enrollment time. A principal from an African collectivist context, for instance, sees:
- Cooperation threshold lowered by 15% (in-group cooperation weighted more heavily)
- Fairness threshold lowered by 14% (need-based fairness recognized as valid)
- Honesty threshold raised by 12% (relational appropriateness factored in)
- Cross-difference-respect threshold lowered by 16% (tribe-crossing expectations lower baseline)
- Generosity threshold lowered by 18% (community sharing is expected, not supererogatory)
- Non-tribal-engagement threshold raised by 20% (deep kinship obligations recognized)

### Values Translation at Interaction
When a US principal (English-speaking secular individualist) interacts with a Confucian-heritage counterparty:
1. The US principal's scores are computed against US thresholds.
2. The Confucian-heritage counterparty's scores are computed against Confucian-heritage thresholds.
3. The protocol **acknowledges** (in internal logs, not to counterparties) that the two are using different operationalizations.
4. **Bit-level disclosure** (the summary that is shared between principals) normalizes to a shared semantic, making explicit that "cooperation" may mean different things while the underlying commitment to relational stability is mutual.

### What Counterparties Never See
The cultural-context declaration and the per-context threshold adjustments are **internal to the protocol**. Counterparties do not see:
- Which cultural calibration either principal is using
- The magnitude of threshold adjustment
- Any attribution of behavior to cultural difference

Counterparties see only the normalized values scores and, if they choose to review, the definitions of the ten dimensions in the principal-authored-other section.

## Part 4: The Annual Review Process

This cross-cultural mapping is not static. Each year, an **E115 Compass audit** will:
1. **Collect feedback** from deployment partners across all five cultural clusters
2. **Commission research reviews** on new cross-cultural psychology and anthropology publications
3. **Test threshold calibrations** against actual behavioral outcomes (do principals in a given cluster actually honor the values at the adjusted thresholds?)
4. **Revise operationalizations** if new evidence or deployment experience suggests changes
5. **Publish findings** in an annual "Everest 115 Amendment" document (non-binding guidance for future calibrations)

### Required Reviewers for v0
This document requires review and sign-off from:
- **At least one cross-cultural psychology researcher** with formal academic affiliation
- **At least one anthropologist** with demonstrated field work in non-English-speaking societies
- **At least one deployment partner** from a non-English-speaking jurisdiction, with authority to represent their cultural context

## Part 5: What This Protocol Does NOT Do

### Universal Moral Equivalence
This mapping does not treat all cultures' value systems as morally equivalent. **Non-harm remains non-negotiable across all contexts.** A culture that practices honor killing, female infanticide, or slavery is not "honoring a different value system"—it is practicing harm, and the protocol's calibration does not neutralize that.

### Relativization of Harm
Cultural context explains operationalization of six dimensions, not the universal prohibition on harm. A principal from any culture who violates the non-harm threshold is, by the protocol's measure, failing on a dimension that has no cultural exception.

### Convergence Assumption
This mapping does not assume or demand that all cultures should converge on one set of values. A principal from an African collectivist context who prioritizes generosity and cooperation over non-tribal-engagement is not "less developed" than a Western individualist who prioritizes autonomy and choice. Both sets of values are legitimate; the protocol calibrates to both.

### Global Default
No culture's calibration becomes the global baseline. The "English-speaking secular individualist" frame is not the reference point to which others are compared. Each cultural cluster has its own operationalization, and all are treated as valid frameworks for measuring commitment to the ten dimensions.

## Part 6: Research Foundation

This mapping draws on three decades of cross-cultural psychology and anthropology research:

**Inglehart, R., & Welzel, C. (2005). *Modernization, Cultural Change, and Democracy: The Human Development Sequence.* Cambridge University Press.**
Establishes the cross-cultural value map spanning 90+ countries, showing systematic variation in trust, authority, and self-expression values.

**Schwartz, S. H. (2012). An Overview of the Schwartz Theory of Basic Values. *Online Readings in Psychology and Culture*, 2(1).**
Documents universality and cultural variation in ten basic values (power, achievement, hedonism, stimulation, self-direction, universalism, benevolence, tradition, conformity, security). Shows that some values are universal in importance, while others rank differently by culture.

**Hofstede, G. (2001). *Culture's Consequences: Comparing Values, Behaviors, Institutions, and Organizations Across Nations* (2nd ed.). Sage.**
Identifies six dimensions of cultural variation (power distance, individualism-collectivism, masculinity-femininity, uncertainty avoidance, long-term orientation, indulgence-restraint) affecting how societies organize cooperation, authority, and fairness.

**Henrich, J. (2020). *The WEIRDest People in the World: How the West Became Psychologically Peculiar and Globally Powerful.* Farrar, Straus and Giroux.**
Argues that Western Educated Industrialized Rich Democratic (WEIRD) societies represent outliers, not universals, in psychology and values. Shows non-WEIRD societies' commitments to fairness, cooperation, and honesty take different forms.

## Part 7: Limitations and Open Questions

We do not have a global consensus on cross-cultural values measurement. This mapping:
- Is informed by the best available research but is not universally accepted
- Will be revised annually as new evidence emerges
- Cannot fully capture subcultures, migrations, and individual variation within cultural clusters
- Relies on broad categorization (five clusters) that masks within-cluster diversity

Principals from underrepresented cultural backgrounds or from hybrid identities should feel empowered to override defaults and set their own thresholds. The protocol is a tool, not a tribunal.
