## Overview: Governance at the Threshold

E215 documented why a treaty-shaped governance is needed — the cryptographic floors (refusal-floor preservation, principal authorship, non-extraterritorial vault posture) require state-actor commitment that licensing + audit panels cannot guarantee alone. E216 brings those abstract floors into the room.

The convening is NOT a negotiation toward a binding treaty. It is a structured deliberation among ≥5 stakeholder classes to validate that the E215 draft is coherent, politically addressable, and legible to its intended audiences (foreign-ministry cyber desks, civil-society organizations, academic cryptographers, disability-rights advocates, international-law scholars).

Output is a revised E215 draft, a published synthesis of stakeholder objections + resolutions, and a commitment schedule for soft-law adoption (OECD / Council of Europe) and formal treaty negotiation (years 3–8 per E215 §5).

## Stakeholder Composition (≥5 Classes, ≥2 Per Class)

Attendance is by invitation. Criteria for inclusion: demonstrated expertise, standing in the relevant constituency, and ability to speak authentically (not as state representative, not as paid consultant, but as stakeholder). Dual enrollment across classes is permitted.

### Academic Crypto (≥2)

- **Cryptographer 1.** Published ZK-proof or post-quantum researcher. Evaluates the technical soundness of the treaty's Articles 5 (cryptographic agility) and the underlying Calm Witness protocol architecture (E1–E305).
- **Cryptographer 2.** Second specialized voice — NIST PQC committee liaison, lattice cryptography expert, or applied ZK-circuit designer. Ensures diversity of technical perspective and catches omissions in the protocol specification.

Charge: Validate that the refusal-floor predicates (E146–E200) do not traffic in cryptographic backdoors; confirm the post-quantum migration plan (E298) is feasible within the treaty's timeline constraints.

### AI Safety (≥2)

- **AI-Safety Researcher 1.** Published work on value alignment or principal-agent problems. Evaluates the coherence of the alignment predicates (E126–E145) and the treaty's Article 6 (principal authorship) as a defense against value-laundering attacks (E274).
- **AI-Safety Researcher 2.** Specialist in misuse, deception detection, or cooperative-game theory. Ensures the treaty's dispute-resolution procedures (E215 §3) do not create perverse incentives for defection or strategic ambiguity.

Charge: Assess whether the treaty's arms-length stance (binds states, not operators) leaves dangerous gaps if a non-signatory state operates a Calm-suite-shaped protocol for prohibited purposes and an operator is caught serving both.

### Disability Rights (≥2)

- **Disability-Rights Advocate 1.** Organizational backing from a published disability-rights body (ADAPT, British Disabled People's Council, or equivalent). Evaluates whether the treaty's refusal-floor categories (Article 3, §2 of E215) genuinely protect against cognitive-disability profiling, neurodiversity discrimination, and mental-health instrumentalization.
- **Disability-Rights Advocate 2.** Lived-experience representative (disabled person) OR scholar in disability justice. Ensures the treaty's implementation bodies (the audit panel, Standing Committee) are accessible and that disability-justice values are non-extractive (Everest 88 / 292 obligations honored).

Charge: Validate that no predicate in the v0 vocabulary (E106–E200) creates a surveillance vector for disability status; ensure that the principal-authorship principle (E215 Article 6) is not undermined by state-side reinterpretation of what "evidence" means.

### Multi-Jurisdiction Legal Counsel (≥2)

- **Counsel 1.** Attorney or legal scholar from a non-US jurisdiction (EU, Global South, or Asia-Pacific preferred). Evaluates how the treaty's non-extraterritorial vault posture (Article 4) interacts with data-localization laws, judicial-cooperation treaties, and the counsel's home jurisdiction's export-control regime.
- **Counsel 2.** Attorney or legal scholar from a second distinct jurisdiction. Ensures Articles 3–6 (the substantive floors) are translatable into domestic law without triggering constitutional objections (e.g., due-process concerns, executive-power questions, or conflicts with existing privacy regimes).

Charge: Identify legal obstacles to ratification and propose treaty language adjustments that preserve the refusal-floor intent while reducing domestic political friction. Validate that Article 8's Standing Committee (E215) is a viable dispute-resolution venue under international-law norms.

### Working-Principal Cohorts (≥2)

- **Principal Cohort 1.** Operators and agents using Calm Witness in production or near-production contexts (not hypothetical). Evaluates whether the governance charter (E215) creates operational friction (e.g., audit costs, dispute-resolution overhead, predicate-publication delays) that would push them toward non-compliant deployments.
- **Principal Cohort 2.** A coalition of principals or their representatives (e.g., AI-safety organizations, disability-justice groups, labor unions) whose values are explicitly NOT the "default" Western-individualist stack. Ensures the treaty's predicate vocabulary (E107, E115, E186–E200) does not export one cultural model as universal.

Charge: Validate that the governance overhead is tolerable and that the dispute-resolution procedures do not bias toward early-mover, well-resourced operators. Ensure the treaty honors the covenant that predicates are reauthored under diverse cultural frameworks (Everest 118).

## Facilitation Discipline

The convening is NOT Foundation-led. The Foundation supplies the context (E215 draft, the spec documents E106–E305) but does NOT chair, does NOT determine consensus, and does NOT block objections from becoming part of the public record.

**Independent Facilitator:** A named neutral third party (not Foundation affiliate, not government, not venture-backed) chairs the convening. Role: enforce transparent deliberation, ensure all stakeholder classes are heard, surface areas of consensus and areas of irreducible disagreement.

Candidates for facilitator: mediators from international-law NGOs (Interpeace, Business & Human Rights Resource Centre), academic conflict-resolution specialists, or civil-society leaders with no stake in the Calm suite's success.

**Facilitation Rules:**

1. **Rotating speaking order.** Each stakeholder class speaks in turn; no class monopolizes time.
2. **Structured objection protocol.** Objections are named explicitly ("This phrase in Article 3 creates a loophole") and logged in the public record with the objector's identity and reasoning. Objection-logging is NOT waived by consensus-seeking.
3. **No deletion.** Every objection that cannot be resolved remains in the public record, labeled as contested.
4. **Affinity groups.** Stakeholder-class members may form sub-groups to draft language proposals between plenary sessions, but proposals are attributed and debated publicly.

## Agenda

**Convening Duration:** ≥3 sessions over ≥2 weeks (not a single all-hands day).

**Session 1 (Day 1–2): Review E215 Draft**

- Calm presents E215 article by article.
- Each stakeholder class names high-level concerns without debate (30 minutes per class).
- Facilitator synthesizes into a structured objection list.
- Working drafts of language alternatives proposed (homework for Session 2).

**Session 2 (Day 8–9): Tribunal of Objections**

- Objection-by-objection deliberation. For each objection, the objector explains; E215 author (or delegate) responds; other stakeholder classes weigh in.
- Goal: move objections from "unclear" to "addressed by language revision" or "irreducible disagreement" or "out of scope for this treaty."
- Facilitator produces a revised E215 draft incorporating consensus-language changes.

**Session 3 (Day 15–16): Ratify or Defer**

- Stakeholder classes vote on acceptance of the revised draft OR recommend deferral for further expert refinement.
- Acceptance does NOT require unanimity; it requires ≥4 of 5 stakeholder classes voting YES (majority, not consensus).
- Any objecting class has their dissent published alongside the revised draft.
- Commitment to next steps: soft-law adoption timeline (E215 §5), treaty-negotiation kickoff date.

## Written Consensus Protocol

A "consensus document" is not a single agreed text; it is a structured record of **what is agreed, what is contested, and what is deferred**.

**Format:**

1. **Core Principles (Uncontested).** Articles 1–2, Articles 7–17 (definitions, procedural structure, civil-society participation, soft-law composition). These are accepted by ≥4 of 5 classes.

2. **Substantive Floors (Contested).** Articles 3–6 (the refusal-floor meat). For each article:
   - The revised language.
   - Classes that accept it.
   - Classes that object and their specific objection (in ≤100 words).
   - Facilitator's notation on whether objection is jurisdictional, technical, or values-based.

3. **Unresolved Tensions.** Items that could not be settled in the convening (e.g., "EU legal counsel insists on an explicit data-localization exception; Global South counsel insists on strict non-extraterritorial language"). Deferred to soft-law negotiation or treaty-negotiation phase.

4. **Process Notes.** How each decision was made (voting, consensus-seeking, deferred). Who dissented and why.

## Refusal-Floor Preservation Test (T-E216.1..6)

This convening does NOT amend the refusal floor (E215 Article 3, scope statement from E146–E200). It VALIDATES the floor.

**The floor is preserved if and only if:**

- **T-E216.1:** No stakeholder class successfully argues that a refusal-floor category (law enforcement, employment, insurance, lending, medical, custody, immigration, surveillance, aggregate analytics) should be LIFTED or EXCEPTED.
- **T-E216.2:** Stakeholder objections may request CLARIFICATION of what a floor category means (e.g., "does 'surveillance' include state-side traffic analysis of who is querying which predicates?"), but clarification does NOT loosen the floor.
- **T-E216.3:** Any stakeholder-class majority can propose TIGHTENING the floor (adding new prohibited categories, e.g., "no predictive harm scoring"). Tightening is permitted; loosening is structurally forbidden.
- **T-E216.4:** If a stakeholder class objects that a refusal-floor category is culturally incompatible with their jurisdiction's norms, the objection is published, but the floor does NOT move. Instead, the objecting jurisdiction's ratification process becomes their choice — they may adopt the treaty with a reservation (Article 12, E215) or decline to ratify.
- **T-E216.5:** The Standing Committee (Article 8, E215) inherits the floor as-is from the convening outcome. No future Standing Committee amendment can weaken the floor (Article 13, E215 bars this; amendment requires 2/3 of signatories AND bars weakening).
- **T-E216.6:** If a stakeholder class reveals that they CANNOT accept the floor as stated, they exit the convening process and the outcome is published with their exit and reasoning documented.

## Funding (Transparent; No Per-Stakeholder Honoraria)

The convening is funded by the Calm Foundation + open-source foundations (Open Philanthropy, Future of Life Institute) without per-participant honoraria or bias-inducing incentives.

**Budget Categories:**

- **Venue:** neutral location (Geneva, Singapore, or Mexico City preferred for geographic neutrality). Foundation covers venue rental + A/V.
- **Travel:** Foundation covers economy travel + 3 nights' accommodation for all participants.
- **Facilitation:** Independent facilitator is compensated at a flat daily rate (no contingency on outcome).
- **Catering + accessibility:** All costs borne by Foundation (including live captioning, ASL interpreters, dietary accommodation).
- **Transcription + publishing:** Professional transcription of all sessions; transcripts published within 7 days of convening end.

**NO:**

- Per-participant honoraria (would bias voting).
- Consulting contracts to "validate" the draft (experts offer opinions, not paid conclusions).
- Funding-stream strings (e.g., "Foundation pays for attendance only if you agree the draft is sound").

## Convening Success Criteria (Acceptance)

The convening is deemed successful if:

- ≥3 of 5 stakeholder classes publish formal written acceptance of the revised E215 draft.
- ≥4 of 5 stakeholder classes ratify that the refusal floor (E216.1..6) is preserved.
- ≥2 jurisdiction-counsel reps confirm that Articles 3–4 are translatable into domestic law without constitutional showstoppers.
- The published outcome document includes all objections, all resolutions, and names of all dissenters.
- A soft-law adoption roadmap (OECD / Council of Europe target year) is committed to and published.

**If acceptance is blocked** (i.e., <3 classes accept; refusal floor is breached): the outcome is still published, dissenters are named, and the decision is deferred 12 months for expert refinement. E216 is not repeated; instead, expert task forces (per stakeholder class) refine the contested articles offline.
