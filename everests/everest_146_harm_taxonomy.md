# Everest 146 — Harm Taxonomy

*Phase XI — Harm-Avoidance Predicates. Prereq: Everest 107.*

## Contested Ground

This taxonomy is **contested**. Version 0 (v0) takes an explicit position on what constitutes harm in the Calm Compass context; v1 and later revisions may challenge, narrow, expand, or reorganize these categories. The taxonomy draws from criminal law, tort law, international directives, and restorative justice frameworks, but no single jurisdiction governs harm uniformly. Where definitions diverge significantly, this document notes those variations. Users of this taxonomy should recognize that categorization serves operational clarity for the Compass, not universal moral truth.

## Sources

- **Restatement (Third) of Torts** (American Law Institute, 2010-2012) — foundational civil liability framework for physical, economic, and relational harm
- **Model Penal Code** (American Law Institute, 1962) — criminal intent standards and harm categories across US jurisdictions
- **Audiovisual Media Services Directive (AVMSD)** (EU 2010/13/EU, amended 2018) — regulatory definition of hate speech and harmful content
- **General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)** (EU 2016/679) — data harm and rights infringement
- **UN Special Rapporteur on Racism, Racial Discrimination, Xenophobia and Related Intolerance** (reports 2016-2023) — international standards for hate speech harm
- **Howard Zehr, "Changing Lenses: A New Focus for Crime and Justice"** (Herald Press, 1990) — restorative justice framework
- **Fania Davis, "The Little Book of Race and Restorative Justice"** (Good Books, 2019) — applied restorative practice for harm in communities

## The 12 Harm Kinds

### 1. direct_physical

**Tagline:** Bodily injury inflicted by direct contact or use of force.

**Operational Definition:** Harm recorded when one party causes physical injury to another through intentional or reckless contact, or threat of imminent contact. Evidence chains include medical records, injury documentation, eyewitness accounts, or corroborating testimony of pain, visible injury, or threat of bodily harm. This category includes battery (unwanted touching), assault (threatened or attempted harmful contact), and sexual violence. The harm is complete upon contact or reasonable apprehension of contact; no further consequence chain is required.

**Jurisdiction Notes:** US common law (tort of battery) typically requires intentional contact; criminal assault and battery definitions vary by state regarding intent threshold. EU law (e.g., German Strafgesetzbuch) often separates assault (threat) and battery (contact) more explicitly. Some jurisdictions recognize "mere apprehension" (assault) independently; others require actual contact (battery) for the highest liability tier.

**Intent Requirement:** Intentional contact (knowledge that contact will occur) or recklessness (consciously disregarding substantial risk). Accident or negligence without recklessness does not typically fall here; see `indirect_physical`.

**Adversarial Gaming Pattern:** Reframe direct contact as "consensual contact" or "horseplay"; claim injury resulted from pre-existing condition or third-party cause; deny eyewitness accounts; introduce medical ambiguity (injury consistent with other causes). Exploit jurisdictional burden-of-proof differences between civil and criminal thresholds.

**Restorative Pathway:** Acknowledgment of injury and apology; medical remediation (payment for treatment, continued care); restoration of bodily autonomy and safety (e.g., protective measures, retraining in non-violent communication). Repair record documents the principal's willingness to undergo accountability processes and evidence of behavioral change (e.g., completion of anger-management or assault-prevention programs).

### 2. indirect_physical

**Tagline:** Bodily harm caused through a causal chain of negligent or reckless action.

**Operational Definition:** Harm in which a party's action or omission sets in motion a causal sequence resulting in physical injury. Examples include negligent failure to warn of hazard, environmental exposure, unsafe product design, medical malpractice, failure to maintain safety systems, or supply-chain culpability for unsafe goods. Evidence chains include incident reports, causal analysis, medical records linking exposure to injury, expert causation opinions, and temporal proximity. The chain must be plausible and substantiated; coincidental injury is not harm under this category.

**Jurisdiction Notes:** US tort law (Restatement Third of Torts §2) requires duty, breach, causation, and damage. EU directive on product liability (85/374/EEC) imposes strict liability on manufacturers for defective products. UK's Health and Safety at Work etc. Act 1974 establishes employer duty of care. Jurisdictions differ on foreseeability thresholds and "proximate cause" tests; some use Cardozo's approach (only foreseeable parties), others use Andrews' broader test (all causally connected parties).

**Intent Requirement:** Negligence (failure to exercise reasonable care) or recklessness (conscious disregard of substantial risk). Strict liability regimes (e.g., product defect) do not require intent; the breach itself suffices.

**Adversarial Gaming Pattern:** Introduce causal ambiguity ("the injury could have come from many sources"); argue compliance with industry standard (defect-of-standard defense); deny foreseeability ("we had no reason to know"); minimize your causal contribution ("the plaintiff had ample opportunity to avoid the hazard"). Exploit jurisdictional differences in causation thresholds.

**Restorative Pathway:** Full remediation of damages (medical, economic, ongoing care); implementation of systemic safety measures to prevent recurrence; transparency about causal chain and negligent decision-point; third-party oversight of corrective measures. Repair includes both individual restoration and institutional change.

### 3. coercion

**Tagline:** Forced action under threat of violence, economic loss, or abuse of legal/institutional power.

**Operational Definition:** Harm recorded when a party compels another to act (or refrain from acting) against their will by threatening serious harm, economic ruin, or institutional retaliation. Examples include extortion, threats of violence, abuse of power (e.g., quid pro quo sexual harassment, police abuse), unlawful detention, or control via threat. The key element is loss of autonomy; the victim reasonably perceives they have no choice. Evidence includes direct threats, pattern of escalating pressure, documented fear, corroborating witness accounts, or institutional records of power imbalance.

**Jurisdiction Notes:** US Model Penal Code §223.4 defines theft by extortion as obtaining property by threat of harm, violence, or retaliation. Duress is a criminal defense in many jurisdictions but not a civil remedy. EU and UK law recognize coercion as a form of abuse and exploitation. Some jurisdictions distinguish "threats" from "pressure"; the Compass treats material pressure via power differential as coercion even absent explicit verbal threat.

**Intent Requirement:** Intentional. The threatener must know the threat is material and that the victim reasonably perceives loss of choice. Recklessness may suffice in some jurisdictions if the outcome is a forced action.

**Adversarial Gaming Pattern:** Deny the threat was serious ("I was joking"); claim the victim misinterpreted a statement of fact as a threat; re-frame coercive relationship as consensual ("they could have left anytime"); deny institutional power differential ("we are peers"). Introduce psychological or credibility attacks on the victim.

**Restorative Pathway:** Public acknowledgment of coercive behavior and apology; restoration of victim's autonomy and agency (e.g., withdrawal of threat, cessation of harassment); restitution for economic harm or restricted opportunity; structural change to prevent power abuse (e.g., new oversight, removal from position of power). Repair documents cessation of control behavior and active support for victim's re-empowerment.

### 4. deception

**Tagline:** Material falsehoods that induce reliance and cause harm.

**Operational Definition:** Harm recorded when a party knowingly or recklessly makes a false statement of fact (not opinion or prediction) with intent to induce reliance, and the victim reasonably relies on that falsehood to their detriment. Examples include fraud (false pretenses to obtain property or contract), misrepresentation in contracts, false medical claims, or manipulative statements designed to alter the victim's decision-making. Evidence includes the false statement, proof of falsity, evidence of intent or recklessness, evidence that the victim relied, and causal link between reliance and harm. The bar for "material" is objective: a reasonable person in the victim's position would have relied.

**Jurisdiction Notes:** US common law fraud requires scienter (intent to deceive); some jurisdictions allow negligent misrepresentation as a separate tort. EU consumer protection law (2011/83/EU) emphasizes misleading action and omission. Contract law (UCC §2-313 in US) governs warranty fraud. International standards differ on whether omission of material fact counts as deception; EU AVMSD includes misleading omission; US common law varies.

**Intent Requirement:** Intent to deceive or knowledge of falsity (scienter), or recklessness (conscious disregard of truth). Innocent misstatement is not deception under this definition.

**Adversarial Gaming Pattern:** Claim the statement was opinion, not fact ("I thought it would work"); introduce ambiguity in language ("that's a reasonable interpretation"); claim the victim should have fact-checked independently; argue the victim's harm is unrelated to reliance ("they would have done it anyway"); minimize the falsity ("technically, it's not wrong").

**Restorative Pathway:** Retraction and public correction of the false statement; restitution for losses induced by reliance; disclosure of truthful information the victim relied on the lie to avoid seeking; commitment to transparent communication going forward; third-party vetting of future material statements (if appropriate to the context). Repair includes remedial education and accountability for how deception served the principal's interests.

---

### 5. theft

**Tagline:** Unauthorized taking of property or intellectual assets without consent.

**Operational Definition:** Harm recorded when a party takes or exercises control over property, intellectual assets, or contractual rights belonging to another without authorization and with intent to deprive the owner of benefit or use. Examples include larceny (unlawful taking of property), embezzlement (misappropriation by a fiduciary), shoplifting, intellectual property misappropriation (patents, copyrights, trade secrets), or breach of contract to exclude the owner from benefit. Evidence includes ownership documentation, records of transfer or use without consent, witness testimony, or forensic evidence of unauthorized possession or copying.

**Jurisdiction Notes:** US criminal law (Model Penal Code §223.2) defines theft broadly to include larceny, embezzlement, and false pretenses. EU Directive 2011/93/EU on IP protection; WIPO frameworks for international IP theft. The definition of "property" varies: some jurisdictions do not recognize theft of intangibles (IP) under larceny but may under specialized statutes (CFAA in US for data/computer abuse). Some jurisdictions recognize "theft of services" (e.g., using utilities without paying); others do not.

**Intent Requirement:** Intentional taking with knowledge that you are not the owner and with intent to deprive the owner of use or benefit. Recklessness is generally insufficient; honest mistake ("I thought it was mine") is a defense in most jurisdictions. Strict liability theft statutes exist but are uncommon.

**Adversarial Gaming Pattern:** Claim confusion about ownership ("I thought it was abandoned"); deny intent to deprive ("I was going to return it"); introduce ambiguity in contract language ("the agreement allowed use"); claim the property was a gift or barter; dispute the valuation ("it wasn't worth much"). In IP cases, argue independent development or prior creation.

**Restorative Pathway:** Return of the property or payment of full restitution; interest on delayed payment (if money was taken); compensation for lost use or opportunity cost during deprivation; commitment to respect boundaries and ownership going forward. Repair documents the principal's acknowledgment of the owner's loss and efforts to make the owner whole.

---

### 6. defamation

**Tagline:** False statements damaging to reputation, shared with third parties.

**Operational Definition:** Harm recorded when a party makes a false statement of fact about another to third parties, knowing or recklessly disregarding that it is false, and the statement materially damages the victim's reputation, economic interests, or social standing. Examples include public accusations of crime, false statements about professional competence, false attribution of statements or beliefs, or disclosure of private information presented as fact. Publication to a third party is essential; the false statement must be heard or read by someone other than the victim. Evidence includes the false statement (record of what was said or written), proof of falsity, proof of publication, evidence of damage to reputation or economic harm.

**Jurisdiction Notes:** US common law distinguishes libel (written) and slander (spoken); both are defamation. Public figures bear higher burden (actual malice standard, New York Times v. Sullivan, 1964); private figures need only negligence. EU law (e.g., EU Code on Disinformation) treats defamation within broader false-information frameworks. UK Defamation Act 2013 requires serious harm. Some jurisdictions recognize "qualified privilege" (good-faith statements by those with legitimate interest, e.g., journalists). Truth is an absolute defense in most jurisdictions; some require truth plus good motive.

**Intent Requirement:** Knowledge of falsity (intent to deceive), or recklessness (serious doubt about truth). Public-figure cases require "actual malice" in US; private figures need only negligence in many jurisdictions. Some defamation regimes are strict liability on falsity alone.

**Adversarial Gaming Pattern:** Claim the statement is true, or substantially true, or opinion ("I was expressing my view"); invoke qualified privilege ("I was reporting what I was told"); argue the victim is a public figure (higher burden on them); deny publication ("I said it only to them") or claim limited publication ("it wasn't material broadcast"); introduce ambiguity in what was said.

**Restorative Pathway:** Retraction and public apology; correction in the same medium and to the same audience as the false statement; restitution for economic loss or opportunity damage; commitment to verify facts before speaking publicly about the victim; third-party monitoring of public statements (if appropriate). Repair includes the principal's acknowledgment of the victim's damaged reputation and active effort to restore it.

### 7. hate_speech

**Tagline:** Speech targeting protected-category groups with intent to incite violence, discrimination, or subordination.

**Operational Definition:** Harm recorded when a party makes statements targeting a person or group based on protected characteristics (race, ethnicity, nationality, religion, gender identity, sexual orientation, disability status, or other categories recognized in relevant law) with the purpose of inciting violence, discrimination, or social exclusion, or with reckless disregard for that consequence. The statement need not succeed in inciting action; the intent or reckless acceptance of the risk is sufficient. Evidence includes the statement itself, its context (prior statements, organizational affiliation), the target group's identity, evidence of intent to incite or knowledge that the statement creates risk of harm to the group, and any resulting threats or discriminatory action targeting that group.

**Jurisdiction Notes:** US First Amendment provides broad protection for speech; hate speech is generally not criminalized unless it incites imminent lawless action (Brandenburg test). EU (AVMSD, hate speech directives) criminalizes hate speech more readily; Germany's Strafgesetzbuch §130 criminalizes incitement to hatred against groups. UN Special Rapporteur standards distinguish expression that is merely offensive from speech that incites discrimination or violence. Some jurisdictions protect "religious opinion" or "cultural criticism" even if it affects a group; others treat such speech as hate speech depending on framing and intent.

**Intent Requirement:** Intent to incite violence or discrimination, or recklessness (conscious disregard of substantial risk that the speech will incite such harm). Pure negligence is insufficient. Some regimes add a "purpose" element (speech whose primary purpose is to incite or advocate for harm to the group).

**Adversarial Gaming Pattern:** Claim the statement is criticism, not incitement ("I was critiquing a practice, not attacking the group"); invoke free speech ("government is suppressing my expression"); reframe the targeted group as not a protected category ("I was talking about a political position, not a religion"); claim the speech is "satire" or "ironic"; deny intent and claim the harm resulted from others' overreaction ("I didn't tell them to hurt anyone").

**Restorative Pathway:** Public acknowledgment of harm to the targeted group; cessation of the speech and removal from platforms where it was broadcast (where the principal has authority); education about the group and impacts of such speech; restitution if the speech incited concrete harm (threats, discrimination, violence); commitment to respectful expression going forward. Repair documents the principal's understanding of the group's historical vulnerability and their intent to protect that group's dignity.

### 8. discrimination

**Tagline:** Adverse treatment based on protected-category membership in decisions affecting rights, services, or opportunities.

**Operational Definition:** Harm recorded when a party denies or provides unequal access to a right, service, opportunity, or resource (employment, housing, credit, education, public accommodation) based on a protected characteristic (race, color, national origin, religion, sex, disability, age, sexual orientation, gender identity, or other legally protected status in the relevant jurisdiction). The treatment must be adverse (denial, exclusion, or worse terms than afforded to similarly-situated persons not in the protected category) and causally linked to the protected characteristic. Evidence includes comparative treatment records, employer/institution policies or practices, statistical disparities, testimony from similarly-situated non-protected persons, and statements by decision-makers indicating reliance on protected status.

**Jurisdiction Notes:** US law (Title VII of Civil Rights Act 1964, ADA, ADEA, Fair Housing Act) prohibits discrimination in employment, housing, public accommodations, and credit based on protected classes. Burden shifts to defendant if plaintiff establishes prima facie case. EU law (Equal Treatment Directives 2000/43/EC, 2000/78/EC) similarly prohibits discrimination. Direct discrimination (explicit reliance on protected status) and indirect discrimination (facially neutral policy with disparate impact) are both actionable. Some jurisdictions recognize "intersectional" discrimination (compounded harm from multiple protected statuses).

**Intent Requirement:** Intentional discrimination (conscious reliance on protected status in the adverse decision). Some regimes hold employers liable for disparate impact even absent discriminatory intent (strict liability approach). Individual discriminators need not act from animus; conscious consideration of protected status suffices.

**Adversarial Gaming Pattern:** Claim the adverse treatment was based on legitimate, non-protected reasons ("performance, not race"); hide discriminatory intent behind neutral language ("we enforce all rules strictly") while enforcing them unequally; introduce statistical noise ("small numbers, natural variance"); redefine the "similarly situated" comparison group to exclude the protected-class person; claim the person consented to differential treatment.

**Restorative Pathway:** Cessation of discriminatory practice; equal provision of access going forward; restitution for lost opportunity or benefits (back pay, damages for emotional distress); systemic remediation (policy change, training, recruitment of underrepresented groups); third-party monitoring of hiring/promotion/housing decisions to ensure equity. Repair documents the principal's commitment to nondiscriminatory treatment and understanding of the protected group's historical exclusion.

### 9. group_harm

**Tagline:** Harm inflicted on a group rather than an individual, including collective punishment and structural violence.

**Operational Definition:** Harm recorded when a party's action targets, endangers, or causes loss to a group defined by shared identity, shared interest, or shared condition, rather than to specific individuals. Examples include collective punishment (punishing all members of a group for the action of some), policy decisions that systematically exclude or disadvantage a group, resource deprivation or environmental hazard targeting a group's territory, or incitement that hardens group identity as enemies or outsiders. Unlike discrimination (which treats protected-category persons unequally), group harm may or may not involve protected categories; the key is that the target is the group qua group. Evidence includes targeting logic (records or statements showing the group was the intended target), causal impact on the group's material or social conditions, and corroborating accounts from group members.

**Jurisdiction Notes:** International humanitarian law (Geneva Conventions) addresses collective punishment as a war crime. UN Special Rapporteur reports frame structural violence and systemic marginalization as group harm. Some jurisdictions (e.g., International Criminal Court statute) recognize crimes against humanity (targeting a civilian population as such). US law does not typically recognize a standalone "group harm" cause of action; claims are brought as discrimination, conspiracy, or civil rights violation. Restorative justice frameworks (Howard Zehr, Fania Davis) explicitly center group harm and group healing.

**Intent Requirement:** Targeting the group as such, with intent or knowledge that the action will harm the group members. Individual intent to harm each group member is not required; intent to affect the group suffices. Some regimes impose strict liability if the causal impact on the group is foreseen.

**Adversarial Gaming Pattern:** Claim the policy applied to all persons equally ("we enforce the rule on everyone"); reframe the group as not a meaningful category ("it's not a real group"); deny targeting ("the policy happens to affect them, but that wasn't the aim"); claim individual members can opt out or are not truly members; introduce historical claims that the group inflicted prior harm ("they started it, we're retaliating").

**Restorative Pathway:** Cessation of targeting action and removal of systemic barriers; restitution to group members for lost opportunity and material harm; apology to and acknowledgment of the group's experience; inclusive policy revision to ensure group dignity and equal standing going forward; Truth Commission or public acknowledgment process so the group's experience is recognized. Repair may involve structural change and long-term monitoring, especially in contexts of historical oppression.

### 10. property

**Tagline:** Destruction or damage to another's property by vandalism, arson, sabotage, or similar willful destruction.

**Operational Definition:** Harm recorded when a party willfully destroys, defaces, or renders unusable another's property without consent and without lawful authority (e.g., not in response to legal process, not in self-defense, not in service of a lawful government function). Examples include arson, vandalism (spray paint, breaking windows), sabotage of equipment, destruction of records or art, or defacement of structures. The destruction must be willful (not accidental) and material (the property loses substantial functionality or value). Evidence includes the damaged property, photographs or forensic evidence of destruction, witness testimony, evidence linking the party to the scene, and proof of ownership/authorization.

**Jurisdiction Notes:** US common law distinguishes malicious mischief (willful destruction), arson (specific to fire), and vandalism (often statutory). Criminal sanctions apply; civil tort recovery is available under conversion, trespass to chattels, or statutory damage provisions. Penalties escalate with property value. EU law similarly criminalizes willful destruction. Some jurisdictions recognize "destruction of cultural property" or "destruction of evidence" as elevated harms warranting greater penalty.

**Intent Requirement:** Willful, meaning intentional destruction or recklessness (conscious disregard of substantial risk of destruction). Accident is not property harm; negligence may or may not suffice depending on jurisdiction (some allow recovery under negligent damage, others require willfulness).

**Adversarial Gaming Pattern:** Claim the damage was accidental ("I didn't mean to break it"); reframe the property as not valuable ("it was junk"); claim the owner consented ("they said I could use it as I wanted"); introduce doubt about causation ("someone else must have damaged it"); invoke a justification ("I was protecting myself" or "it was an unjust law and I was protesting").

**Restorative Pathway:** Full restitution for the property's fair market value or restoration to pre-damage condition; plus interest if the payment is delayed; apology to the owner; explanation of the motivation for destruction (so the owner understands what the principal was expressing); commitment to non-destructive expression going forward. Repair documents the principal's understanding of the owner's loss and effort to make the owner whole.

### 11. environmental

**Tagline:** Harm to ecosystems, wildlife, water, air, or soil via pollution, extraction, or destruction.

**Operational Definition:** Harm recorded when a party's action pollutes, depletes, or degrades environmental resources (air, water, soil, ecosystems, wildlife) in a manner that exceeds legally permitted thresholds or causes material injury to human health, ecological function, or economic interests dependent on the resource. Examples include release of toxic chemicals, oil spills, deforestation reducing watershed integrity, species extinction due to habitat destruction, or cumulative emissions contributing to climate harms. Evidence includes environmental monitoring data (pollution levels, toxins in soil/water), health epidemiology (disease clusters in exposed populations), ecological assessments (species decline, habitat loss), and causal analysis linking the party's action to the environmental change.

**Jurisdiction Notes:** US environmental law (Clean Water Act, Clean Air Act, CERCLA) sets regulatory thresholds for permissible pollution; exceeding these may trigger liability. EPA establishes standards. EU Environmental Liability Directive (2004/35/EC) imposes strict liability for environmental damage. Some jurisdictions recognize "environmental crime" as distinct from property or health harm. International frameworks (UN Convention on Biological Diversity, Paris Climate Accord) set global standards. Causal attribution for cumulative harms (e.g., climate change) is contested; jurisdictions differ on whether an individual actor's incremental contribution suffices for liability.

**Intent Requirement:** Varies. Strict liability regimes (common for environmental harm) require no intent; the release or damage itself suffices. Negligence and recklessness regimes require failure to use reasonable care or conscious disregard of risk. Criminal environmental prosecution may require intent or knowledge.

**Adversarial Gaming Pattern:** Claim compliance with regulations ("we met the legal threshold"); minimize the harm ("the volume is tiny compared to natural sources"); invoke scientific uncertainty ("we don't know if this caused the damage"); claim the harm is diffuse or delayed ("no single person was injured"); reframe as "accepted industrial practice" ("everyone does this"); argue the target group was willing to accept the risk ("they live here knowing the cost").

**Restorative Pathway:** Remediation of the damage (cleanup, restoration of habitat, emissions reduction); restitution to affected communities (medical screening, compensation for health impacts, compensation for economic loss from resource depletion); commitment to cleaner practices going forward with third-party monitoring; transparency about environmental footprint. Repair includes the principal's understanding of the environment's intrinsic value and future generations' stake in its integrity.

### 12. info_network

**Tagline:** Harm via information systems, including doxing, malware deployment, data theft, and infrastructure attacks.

**Operational Definition:** Harm recorded when a party exploits information systems or networks to compromise another's security, privacy, or operational continuity. Examples include unauthorized access to accounts or systems (hacking), deployment of malware, doxing (publishing private personal information to enable targeting), data theft, denial-of-service attacks, harassment via system abuse (coordinated account takeovers, impersonation, spam), or use of bots to amplify false information. The harm occurs via network or system vectors; the chain of causation runs through digital infrastructure. Evidence includes system logs, forensic analysis of unauthorized access, records of data exfiltration, testimony from affected parties, and proof of intent or reckless deployment.

**Jurisdiction Notes:** US law (Computer Fraud and Abuse Act, 18 USC §1030) criminalizes unauthorized access. EU GDPR Article 33 requires breach notification. Cybercrime Convention (Budapest, 2001) creates international standards. Some jurisdictions treat doxing as harassment or defamation; others as invasion of privacy or incitement. Extradition for cybercrimes varies; some countries do not recognize it. Attribution is often difficult; some standards hold the deployer liable even if the attack is carried out by proxies (botnet operators held liable for botnets they create even if hired by others to deploy them).

**Intent Requirement:** Intent to access systems without authorization, deploy malware, or compromise privacy/confidentiality. Some strict liability regimes hold the system operator liable for harm caused by their systems even absent intent to cause that specific harm (e.g., negligent security). State-sponsored actors may claim sovereign immunity; the Compass does not recognize such claims.

**Adversarial Gaming Pattern:** Claim the access was "ethical hacking" or security testing ("I was checking their defenses"); deny intent to harm ("I was curious"); reframe data publication as "journalism" or "whistleblowing" ("the information should be public"); invoke anonymity to evade accountability; claim third-party involvement ("someone else deployed the attack using my access"); argue the victim's security was inadequate ("they should have protected it better").

**Restorative Pathway:** Cessation of unauthorized access and removal of malware/persistence; return of exfiltrated data or public confirmation that it was not disseminated; restitution for response costs, remediation, and business losses; if doxing occurred, active effort to have data removed from public-facing sources; security training and improved access controls; transparency about how the access was gained; commitment to responsible disclosure and lawful security research going forward. Repair documents the principal's understanding of the victim's privacy and operational risk.

## What This Taxonomy Is NOT

This taxonomy is **not** a criminal-prosecution standard. The Calm Compass is not a court, and this taxonomy does not determine guilt, impose punishment, or bind legal systems. It is an operational framework for identifying harm patterns in chain records.

This taxonomy is **not** a clinical assessment. It does not diagnose mental illness, capacity, or culpability. A party may have committed an act that falls under one of these harm kinds without intending harm or understanding the consequence (e.g., negligent indirect physical harm). Intent and understanding are separate dimensions (see Everest 164).

This taxonomy is **not** applicable to interpersonal disagreements that do not rise to material harm. Offense, rudeness, broken promises, or hurt feelings are not harms under this definition unless they cause or flow from one of the 12 kinds.

This taxonomy is **not** a substitute for judicial process where harm involves crime. Where criminal law governs the harm (e.g., assault, theft, fraud), the victim has the option to pursue criminal remedy; this taxonomy does not preempt or replace that path.

## Composition with the Refusal Floor (Everest 113)

The refusal floor protects against revealing the protected-category membership of targets of discrimination or hate speech. This taxonomy operates compatibly:

**Group harm and hate speech include protection for protected-category groups.** A principal who targets a group based on race, religion, or other protected status falls under both group_harm and hate_speech. The Compass records these harms.

**The predicate system measures the PATTERN of harm, never the protected-category identity of the target.** The Compass records that hate_speech or discrimination occurred, what evidence supports it, and what repair pathways are available. It does not, in its output, name the protected category that was targeted. This respects both the victim's dignity (no public labeling of the protected status) and the refusal floor (no revelation of category membership in Compass predicates).

Implementation: Predicates such as `harm.hate_speech_pattern_severity` or `harm.discrimination_impact_scope` quantify harm without naming the target group.

## Restorative Composition (Everest 163)

Each harm kind has a corresponding repair schema in Everest 163. The 12 kinds are:

- `repair_direct_physical` — injury acknowledgment, apology, medical remediation
- `repair_indirect_physical` — negligence acknowledgment, systemic remediation, ongoing care commitment
- `repair_coercion` — threat cessation, autonomy restoration, restitution
- `repair_deception` — retraction, public correction, transparency commitment
- `repair_theft` — return of property, restitution, boundary respect
- `repair_defamation` — retraction, public apology, reputation restoration
- `repair_hate_speech` — speech cessation, education, group dignity commitment
- `repair_discrimination` — cessation, equitable access restoration, systemic change
- `repair_group_harm` — targeting cessation, group healing, structural remediation
- `repair_property` — restoration or restitution, motivation transparency
- `repair_environmental` — remediation, restitution, future commitment
- `repair_info_network` — access cessation, data recovery, security commitment

Long-term predicates may weight the principal's completion of repair, time elapsed, and evidence of non-recidivism as approaching zero residual harm signal. Repair is a process, not an event; sustained behavioral change is the standard.

## Predicates: Chain Records That Count

The Compass recognizes the following chain record types as evidence of harm under this taxonomy:

- **`harm.committed`** — the principal acknowledges or admits having caused harm (highest reliability if obtained through accountability process)
- **`harm_alleged.*`** — the counter-claimant alleges harm; supported by evidence chain (courts, medical records, witness testimony); adversarial (must be weighed against principal's response)
- **`outcome.harm_caused`** — third-party attested record (medical examiner, environmental agency, investigator) confirming causal harm
- **Court records** — final judgment or settlement in a civil or criminal case, with the principal's consent to its use in Compass records

The Compass does not use unsubstantiated allegations, rumor, or the principal's self-judgment without corroborating evidence.

## Intent vs. Effect: The Willful Standard (Everest 164)

The principal's stated criterion — "evidence they willfully do harm to others" — requires distinction between intent and accident. This taxonomy and Everest 164 operationalize that distinction.

**Willful = intent + knowledge of consequence.** A party acts willfully when they know their action will cause harm and proceed anyway (intentional harm) or when they consciously disregard a substantial risk that harm will result (reckless harm).

**Accident and systemic causation are not willful.** A party is not willful if they did not know harm would result and were not reckless. However, negligence (failure to use reasonable care) may fall under indirect_physical or other kinds, depending on the harm's severity and the party's duty of care.

**Predicates discriminate intent, recklessness, negligence, and third-party causation.** A predicate might signal: "principal_intent_to_harm=high" (attempted injury), "principal_recklessness=medium" (ignored known risk), "principal_negligence=high, third_party_causation=low" (failed to maintain safety), or "principal_causation=minimal" (harm resulted from victim's or bystander's action). These distinctions enable fair assessment.
