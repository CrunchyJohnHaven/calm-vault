## §1 — One-line claim

Between roughly 10:49 ET and 13:08 ET on 2026-05-20, the substrate at `~/.calm-vault/user_state.jsonl` accumulated **103 records covering 52 unique summits, with 42 redundant `summit_bagged` records (45% of all bags) and 4 burst windows**. Multiple CALM-signed sessions ran the same gate scripts in overlapping windows without coordinating their appends.

This is not a cryptographic failure. The chain is hash-valid and every record is schema-conformant. It is an **audit-log clarity** failure: a future verifier reading the chain cannot tell whether 7 records for `summit_bagged{summit_number:124}` mean "the gate passed 7 times" (operationally interesting) or "two parallel sessions both ran the same script three times each plus a chain_repair retry" (an artifact of the operating environment).

## §3 — Why this matters

The chain's stated value (`USER_STATE_PROTOCOL.md` §"Why hash-chained, not just timestamped") is **tamperproof clarity**. Hash chaining gives integrity. Append-only gives history. Together they let a counterparty trust that the principal's narrative is what it says it is.

Redundant `summit_bagged` records do not break either property, but they do erode the third property the protocol implicitly relies on: **single-meaning records**. If a verifier sees `summit_bagged{124}` seven times, the verifier has no protocol-defined way to decide which one is the canonical bag. The audit log is now ambiguous in a way the protocol did not contemplate.

This becomes acute when the chain head is published to Sigsum (Everest 30) or used for any external attestation: an external verifier holding `head_hash = <H>` has no honest answer to "how many summits has this principal bagged?" because the answer depends on how duplicates are counted.

## §7 — Three remediation paths John can pick from

These are choices for the principal, not actions a CALM session should take unilaterally.

### §7.1 — Sequencing mutex on the substrate (lightest)

Wrap every append to `user_state.jsonl` in a per-file lock (`fcntl.flock` or sidecar `.lock`) acquired by the gate script. Concurrent CALM sessions block each other on the file. **Pros:** trivial code change, preserves "any operator can append." **Cons:** does not prevent the same gate from running twice; only prevents interleaved writes. Duplicates can still happen sequentially.

### §7.2 — Per-summit idempotency check in the gate scripts (medium)

Before any gate appends, scan the chain for an existing `summit_bagged{summit_number:N}` with matching `evidence_sha256` set. If found, write a `kind: "summit_re_bag_skipped"` informational record instead (or do not write at all). **Pros:** kills the redundant-bag class entirely; the chain stays a single-meaning record per summit. **Cons:** changes the semantics of "bag" — now bagging is content-addressed by evidence, not by gate-run. Forces a decision: does re-running a gate with new evidence count as a new bag, or as an update? (`COMPASS_EVIDENCE_CEREMONY_v0.md` already has a "supersedes" pattern that maps cleanly here.)

### §7.3 — Canonicalization ratification record (heaviest, most honest)

Add a new record kind `chain_canonicalization` whose payload names a `bag_set` (the canonical mapping of `summit_number` → chosen `seq`) and is two-party-signed by the principal + the operator. All subsequent verifiers MUST consult the latest `chain_canonicalization` record before counting summits. **Pros:** turns the storm into a known historical event with a recorded resolution; future readers see exactly one bag per summit; preserves the full audit trail. **Cons:** requires a schema-version bump (`schema_version: 1`) and a verifier update; cannot be done unilaterally by a single CALM session.

The right answer is probably §7.1 immediately + §7.3 in v1. §7.2 is a structural call about "what does a bag mean" that belongs to the predicate audit panel (`PREDICATE_AUDIT_PROCESS_v0.md`), not to a single pass.
