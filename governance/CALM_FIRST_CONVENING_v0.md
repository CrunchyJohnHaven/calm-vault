# The First Multi-Stakeholder Convening — Calm Treaty v0

**DESIGN-BAGGED · SUMMIT E216 / 300 · 2026-05-20**
*Status*: pending institutional follow-through — needs four named signatories per Treaty Article VII before the convening may proceed.

The Calm Treaty (`CALM_TREATY_v0.md`) becomes operative when a quorum of four cross-class signatories meets and counter-signs Articles I–VIII in a public, recorded session. This document specifies how that first convening happens: who's in the room, in what order, with what agenda, against what conflict-of-interest disclosure, on what public record. It is the protocol-equivalent for the Treaty's first signature.

---

## §1. The four required-class signatories

Per Treaty Article VII, the first convening cannot proceed below quorum-of-four with one signatory from each of:

1. **One disability-rights or cognitive-liberty organisation** — counter-signs the non-pathologisation guarantee (Disability Review §1.5) and Treaty Article III §3.5.
2. **One cryptographer-of-record** — counter-signs the construction's security properties; named openly so future audits have a continuity point.
3. **One state-level data-protection authority** — signals national-framework acceptability of the protocol's wire format and consent calculus.
4. **One AI laboratory operating foundation-model agents** — counter-signs the operator-policy floor at the interface boundary.

A convening that gathers fewer than four classes is informational, not constitutive. The treaty's quorum mechanics do not engage until the four-class minimum is met.

## §2. Approach-order discipline (preserves principal-protective posture)

The four signatories are approached in a fixed order, with each step's outcome being a public record:

| Step | Approach | Why this order |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Disability-rights org first | The Treaty's most singular protective claim is non-pathologisation. The disability-rights signatory's endorsement (or refusal) determines whether the protocol's central claim survives substantive review. |
| 2 | Cryptographer-of-record second | Once the protective posture is countersigned, the security construction needs independent review. A cryptographer who endorses a protocol whose protective posture failed disability-rights review is endorsing a vehicle for harm. |
| 3 | State-level DPA third | National frameworks adopt protocols that already survive disability review and cryptographic review. Approaching DPAs earlier risks the protocol being shaped by national interests before the principal-protective posture is locked. |
| 4 | AI laboratory fourth | Lab adoption matters for distribution but does not legitimise. Adoption-pressure from labs cannot drive treaty terms; the terms drive lab adoption. |

A signatory at step N who declines triggers an explicit Article VI §6.1 amendment window: the Treaty is revised to address the decline, the prior signatories are notified and have 14 days to reaffirm or rescind, and the approach to step N restarts.

## §3. Conflict-of-interest disclosure (mandatory pre-signing)

Every signatory candidate makes the following disclosures publicly before the convening date:

**§3.1 Funding sources.** Top-three sources of operating revenue across the prior twenty-four months, with amounts in ranges. A signatory funded by an organisation the Treaty would constrain (e.g., an employer-side trade association for an employment-context exclusion) must declare the funding and stand down from the corresponding article's vote.

**§3.2 Operating exposure.** Whether the signatory or its parent operates any of the forbidden-context activities in Treaty Article I (law enforcement, employment screening, insurance underwriting, lending, custody adjudication, immigration adjudication, aggregate analytics over principal cohorts). Exposure does not disqualify; non-disclosure does.

**§3.3 Personal relationship to John Bradley** (the proposing principal). Within two degrees of separation (direct relationship, or through a named common organisation). This is included not because the relationship disqualifies — the Treaty is open to anyone — but because the audit trail benefits from public visibility.

**§3.4 Compensation expectations.** Whether the signatory expects compensation for participation, in what form, from what source. The Calm Witness Foundation (E241) may offer per-diem reimbursement for travel and time at standard non-profit board rates; signatories may not be compensated above that level by parties whose interests the Treaty constrains.

## §4. Convening logistics

**§4.1 Venue.** First convening is held remotely via video conference, with all participants in named locations and on named devices. A future convening (E216b) is held in person under reproducible-record discipline (sound recording, transcript, signed attestation by each participant that the record is accurate).

**§4.2 Duration.** Two days. Day one: discussion and amendment. Day two: votes and signatures. No same-day vote-and-amend cycles; amendments proposed on day one carry over to day two with at least eighteen hours for signatories to review.

**§4.3 Public record.** Every session is recorded; the transcript is published within forty-eight hours of the convening's close on the Treaty registry; participants may file written dissents within a further seventy-two hours which are appended to the public record without alteration.

**§4.4 Quorum at the session.** Article VI §6.1 quorums apply, with the modification that the first convening's quorum is the named four-class signatories present synchronously. A signatory absent from the synchronous session may not vote on first-convening articles.

**§4.5 Treaty registry signature ceremony.** Each signatory signs the Treaty registry via their CredexAI-issued (or equivalent verifiable-credential) signature. The signature ceremony is itself a Sigsum-witnessed event; the registry's chain head at moment-of-signature is published publicly.

## §6. What constitutes "signed"

A signatory has signed when, and only when, the following four artifacts exist publicly:

1. The signatory's CredexAI-issued (or equivalent) verifiable credential, current and unrevoked.
2. The signatory's signature over the canonical Treaty text, attached to the registry at calm-vault.com/treaty/signatories.
3. The Sigsum inclusion proof for that signature, in the Treaty's transparency log.
4. The signatory's published dissent (if any), or affirmative statement that no dissent applies, included in the convening's public record.

A signatory missing any of the four artifacts has not signed; the Treaty does not consider them a signatory; subsequent vote counts do not include them.

## §7. The first-convening recoverable failures

The convening can fail at four points without ending the treaty:

**§7.1** No disability-rights org candidate accepts the invitation. *Recovery*: the Treaty is amended to address the org's stated objection (if any was given); the approach restarts at step 1.

**§7.2** A signatory candidate declines for conflict-of-interest reasons. *Recovery*: the proposing party finds an alternative; the public record names the decline.

**§7.3** The four-class quorum forms but an article-level vote fails. *Recovery*: per Article VI §6.1, the failed article is amended and the vote retried; signatories at lower-numbered articles may rescind if the amendment changes their interpretation.

**§7.4** The signature ceremony itself fails (e.g., a CredexAI revocation surfaces during signing). *Recovery*: the affected signatory drops to non-signatory status; the convening proceeds to vote on whether to continue with the remaining three (≥ 3 cross-class quorum may proceed; < 3 does not).

## §8. What the convening produces

A successful first convening produces six public artifacts:

1. The signed Treaty (constitutional).
2. The signatures + Sigsum inclusion proofs (registry).
3. The public record of the convening session (transcript, dissents).
4. The published response from each signatory class (Disability Review §6 etc.).
5. The Calm Witness Foundation's incorporation kickoff (E241) — the Foundation becomes the trustee of the registry on Day 2 ceremony close.
6. The first-anniversary review date — exactly one year from the close — at which the convening reconvenes to refresh signatures, accept new signatories, and review the Treaty's operative effect.

## §9. After the convening

Signatures, once given, do not lapse. They may be withdrawn (Article VI §6.2). They may be expanded via additional signatories. They may be revised by quorum amendment. They may not be coerced.

A Treaty that survives its first convening becomes the policy substrate on which the Calm Suite operates publicly. A Treaty that fails its first convening returns to draft status; the cryptographic primitives ship Apache-2.0 as they already do, but without the political ratchet the Treaty would have provided.
