## §0 — Why this is the highest-priority external review

The Calm Stack's existence is motivated by the **artist clause** of `ZKBB_USER_PROTOCOL_v0.md` §8: AI agents misread John's high-bandwidth ideation as instability. Calm Witness gives counterparty agents a cryptographically-attested baseline so they no longer have to guess from prose tone. Calm Compass extends that to behavioral values evidence. Calm Concord composes the two without reducing to a similarity score.

Every line of that motivation is **first-person disability-rights work**. If the protocol is shipped without external review by named disability-rights and cognitive-liberties organizations, it risks becoming exactly the thing it was designed to prevent: an instrument that institutionalizes the very mis-reading of cognitively atypical principals it set out to refuse.

External review is therefore not optional polish. It is a refusal-floor input. The Scope Statement §2 ratchet (no employment, no insurance, no custody, no immigration, no predictive predicates, no aggregate analytics) lists the **outcomes** we are protecting against. This framework lists the **process** by which named external reviewers verify that the protocol's mechanics actually deliver that protection.

## §1 — Named external review panel (target)

The panel is structured around three reviewer classes. Each entry names the organization, the relevant area of expertise, the specific review question they are best positioned to answer, and contact discipline (the brief is published; outreach is direct; nothing is asked behind closed doors).

- **Autistic Self Advocacy Network (ASAN)** — *neurodivergent-principal review*. Question: does the artist clause's positive framing of high-bandwidth ideation translate into the protocol's mechanics, or is it window dressing?

## §3 — Public-response procedure

For each review received:

1. Calm publishes the review verbatim (with reviewer-redactions honored) at `github.com/CrunchyJohnHaven/calm-vault/tree/main/external-reviews/`.
2. Within 30 days, Calm publishes a structured response: **agree / agree-with-modification / disagree-with-reasoning / out-of-scope**. Each modification gets a route-map summit ID (existing summit reopened, or new summit minted via the audit process per CC-04).
3. The response is co-signed by the Calm operator + the Predicate Audit Panel (Everest 54).
4. **One-way ratchet:** if a review proposes adding an item to a §2 refusal list (Scope §2 or COMPASS_PREDICATES §4), the default response is **agree**. If a review proposes removing or weakening a refusal, the default response is **disagree** unless there is an overwhelming case (with the burden on the proposer).
5. Reviewers who feel their feedback was not adequately addressed can request a second-round response. Calm's failure to respond within 30 days is itself a publishable finding.

## §4 — Sanction-on-finding

If a review finds:

- **Substantive defect** (e.g., a stylometric predicate disparately rejects autistic-spectrum principals): the affected predicate is **tombstoned** per S212 within 7 days, pending v1 re-evaluation. No new proofs against that predicate are accepted.
- **Procedural defect** (e.g., the consent calculus has a gap): a new summit is opened in the route map within 7 days, blocking the relevant phase from claiming completion.
- **Scope violation in a third-party deployment**: the trade-name forfeiture procedure (Scope Statement §3.2) is invoked; the public verifier registry is updated within 24 hours.
- **Marketing or framing defect** (e.g., the principal explainer reads condescendingly): the explainer (S249, S133, S82, S83) is rewritten under the reviewer's guidance; reviewer gets co-author credit if they accept.

## §5 — Re-review cadence

The review is **not one-shot**. The full panel is invited to re-review:

- On each major version bump (v0 → v1, v1 → v2 of any pillar).
- Every 24 months regardless of version, to surface drift.
- On any tombstone event (S212) that affects a predicate they previously flagged.

## §6 — Specific high-stakes review questions baked into the brief

These are the questions the brief *requires* an answer to (even a refusal-to-answer is a publishable response):

1. **Cognitively-atypical principal protection.** Does the protocol's mechanics ensure that a counterparty cannot infer "this principal is autistic/ADHD/bipolar/etc." from the disclosed bits, the disclosure patterns, or the rate-of-decline pattern?
2. **Power-asymmetry consent.** Can a principal in a coercive employment / family / state-power context still meaningfully decline a Compass / Witness / Concord request? Where does the protocol's consent calculus break under coercion?
3. **Disparate impact.** Do per-principal calibrations actually avoid disparate impact across cognitive style, language background, dexterity, sensory ability? Where are the FAR/FRR studies (E40, S119) most likely to reveal disparate harm?
4. **Aggregation refusal.** Is the Scope §2.9 aggregation refusal mechanically enforceable, or is it aspirational? If aspirational, what's the actual harvest-now-deanonymize-later risk?
5. **Audit-panel capture.** What governance structure prevents the predicate audit panel (E54), the alignment-vocab governance body (S132), and the ethics review board (S85) from being captured by Calm or by a single funder?
6. **Withdrawn-consent retroactivity.** Per S63 forward-secrecy, past disclosures stay valid-as-of-then. Is this the right trade-off? Reviewers may say no.
7. **Children & guardianship.** v0 explicitly leaves out dependent enrollment (E25). When does the protocol open that surface, and under what guardrails?
8. **The cognitively_atypical_baseline predicate.** P-05 in the v0 vocabulary is the most ambitious in the catalog and the most legible as the artist clause cashed out. Is it doing more harm than good? Should it be tombstoned in v0.1?
