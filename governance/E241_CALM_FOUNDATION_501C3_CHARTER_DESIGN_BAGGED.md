## §0 — Why a 501(c)(3) and not a B-corp / DAO / LLC

The Calm Suite's social value depends on **non-extractive stewardship**: the registry, the trademarks, the bounty pool, and the conformance certification must outlive the founder and resist commercial capture. A 501(c)(3) Delaware non-profit:

- Has fiduciary duty to public benefit, not to shareholders.
- Cannot be sold; assets revert to another 501(c)(3) on dissolution.
- Donations are tax-deductible to US donors (broader funding pool).
- Has well-established governance norms (board terms, COI rules, public Form 990).

A B-corp retains profit motive and equity holders. A DAO opens governance to token-weighted voting (which the Concord anti-purity-test and refusal floor *cannot* survive). An LLC is owned by members and can be sold. None of the alternatives sustain the structural sovereignty separation specified in [`E215_TREATY_GOVERNANCE_DRAFT_DESIGN_BAGGED.md`](E215_TREATY_GOVERNANCE_DRAFT_DESIGN_BAGGED.md) §4 (Foundation = fiscal-only, NOT sovereign on protocol matters).

Foundation governance therefore commits to **501(c)(3) Delaware non-profit corporation** as the entity form. Alternative forms (Wyoming social-purpose, Swiss verein, CIC) are tracked as fallback options in §10 if Delaware proves unsuitable.

## §2 — Exempt purpose (Form 1023 narrative source)

The Foundation operates exclusively for charitable, educational, and scientific purposes within the meaning of Section 501(c)(3), specifically:

> To advance the privacy, dignity, and self-determination of human principals in their interactions with autonomous AI agents by stewarding the Calm Suite of open-source cryptographic protocols. The Foundation operates the public predicate registry, the public verifier registry, the sanction list, the bounty program, and the conformance certification process for the Calm Suite. The Foundation does NOT set protocol policy; the five primitive-governance sovereigns (Calm Pact Audit Panel, Calm Witness Predicate Audit Panel, Calm Tenancy Conduct Panel, Calm Compass Audit Panel, Calm Concord Acceptable-Purpose Body) hold protocol-level authority. The Foundation supports their work fiscally, holds the trademarks, and operates the public infrastructure that lets the Calm Suite remain a public good.

Charitable activities specifically include: (a) operating public technical infrastructure; (b) funding adversarial security review (bounty program); (c) providing educational materials and curricula (universities, K-12 future, library programs); (d) supporting external review by disability-rights, neurorights, and bioethics scholars; (e) translating the protocol's principal-explainer and counterparty-explainer into ≥10 languages.

## §3 — Board of Directors

### §3.1 — Composition

7 voting board members. Composition rule:

- **≤2 members from any single org** (including founder org Creativity Machine LLC).
- **≥1 member with lived-experience credentials** for cognitive-atypical principal protection.
- **≥1 member with disability-rights advocacy credentials.**
- **≥1 member with cryptographic security expertise** (academic or industry).
- **≥1 member with bioethics or law-and-tech academic appointment.**
- **≥1 member from a founder-independent funder pool.**
- **≥1 member with international civil-society experience** (UN agency, EFF analog, etc.).

### §3.2 — Terms

- 3-year staggered terms; 2 members elected per year (after initial 3-2-2 stagger).
- Two-consecutive-term limit (max 6 years), then 3-year cooling-off.
- Removal: simple majority of remaining board for cause; supermajority (5 of remaining 6) for no-cause.

### §3.3 — Compensation

- Per-meeting honorarium (target $1,000 per board meeting attended, ≤6 meetings/year); annual chair stipend $20,000.
- All compensation publicly disclosed per S220.
- No equity, no profit-share (501(c)(3) prohibition).
- Reimbursement for travel to in-person convenings + treaty conferences.

## §4 — Conflict-of-Interest discipline

Every board member files an annual COI disclosure covering:

- Employment + advisory roles at any Calm-affiliated operator org.
- Equity holdings in for-profit entities deploying Calm-Suite-compliant infrastructure.
- Funding received personally from any source ≥$10k in prior 12 months.
- Family-member positions in Calm-related orgs.

Disclosures are public, posted to `calm-foundation.org/governance/coi`.

Recusal required: any board member discloses a COI on any matter; the recused member leaves the room; vote proceeds. If recusals drop quorum below 4, matter is deferred 30 days for resolution.

**Hard prohibition:** no board member may simultaneously serve on any protocol-level sovereign panel (Pact / Witness / Tenancy / Compass / Concord audit panel). The treaty's sovereigns-vs-Foundation separation is enforced at the board level.

## §5 — What the Foundation does — and does NOT do

### Does:

- Operate the public predicate registry (S127) and verifier registry (S247).
- Operate the sanction list (S216) and audit-transparency log (S222).
- Fund the bounty program (S97; target initial pool ≥$250k from individual donations + foundation grants).
- Fund the conformance certification process (S95) and pay external certifiers.
- Hold the trademarks; enforce them under Scope §3.2.
- Pay per-meeting honoraria for the five sovereign panels (§220 transparency).
- Operate the public website + canonical doc portal.
- File annual Form 990 + state filings.
- Convene the annual treaty meeting (E216) logistics.
- Maintain founder-outlived continuity (E300).

### Does NOT:

- Set protocol policy (sovereign panels do).
- Vote on tombstones, predicates, refusal floors, or purpose taxonomies.
- Accept funding with strings attached that would constrain protocol independence.
- Operate any commercial business (would jeopardize 501(c)(3) status).
- Hold equity in for-profit operators of the Calm Suite.
- Issue any Calm-Suite-name license to a §2 violator (Scope ratchet).
- Endorse one operator over another (registry is neutral).

## §6 — Funding model

### §6.1 — Revenue mix targets

- **35%** Individual donors (recurring + one-time).
- **30%** Foundation grants (Knight, Ford, Open Society, MacArthur, NEH, NSF if eligible).
- **20%** Operator subscription (per S247 registry listing; sliding-scale fee).
- **10%** Conformance certification fees (per S95).
- **5%** Earned program revenue (educational licensing, etc.).

### §6.2 — Funding-source guardrails

- **No single source >33% of annual revenue.** If exceeded, the Foundation publishes a public notice + offers any principal with current chain records an opt-out from registry listing.
- **No state-actor funding** for protocol-policy-affecting line items. State-actor support of conformance certification or interop testing is acceptable; state-actor support of refusal-floor research is acceptable; state-actor funding tied to any predicate-vocabulary decision is refused.
- **No corporate funding from §2-violator deployments.** A would-be donor whose deployment is on the sanction list is refused.
- **Operator subscriptions** sliding-scale: <100 principals served = waived; 100-10k = $200/yr; 10k+ = 1% of operator revenue capped at $50k/yr.

### §6.3 — Reserve

Target 18-month operating reserve invested per Delaware non-profit-investment rules (UPMIFA). Reserve composition: ≥40% short-term Treasuries; remainder in low-fee index funds + small (≤10%) impact investments aligned with charitable purpose.

## §8 — Bylaws ratification + amendment

The bylaws (this document, when adopted) require:

- Founding board ratification by ≥5 of 7 votes within 90 days of incorporation.
- Subsequent amendment by ≥5 of 7 votes + 60-day public-comment window.
- **One-way ratchet** clause: amendments tightening the §5 NOT-list, the §6.2 funding guardrails, or the COI prohibitions require simple majority (4 of 7). Amendments LOOSENING those guards require supermajority (6 of 7) + 90-day cooling-off + public unanimous-justification.

---

## §9 — Dissolution

On dissolution, all assets revert to:

1. **First priority:** another 501(c)(3) operating in the privacy-tech / disability-rights / neurorights space, named by the dissolving board.
2. **Fallback:** EFF (Electronic Frontier Foundation), or successor organization with EFF's mission.
3. **Final fallback:** redistributed to ≥3 disability-rights 501(c)(3)s in proportion to their public Form 990 revenue.

Trademarks: revert to public domain on dissolution, with restrictive scope-statement reservations (Scope §2 ratchet survives the Foundation's dissolution and binds any post-dissolution deployment).

---

## §10 — Fallback entity forms

If Delaware 501(c)(3) is unavailable or proves unsuitable, the Foundation may incorporate as:

1. **Wyoming social-purpose corporation** with mission-locked bylaws (closest US analog).
2. **Swiss verein** (used by IETF, ICANN, NPR) — international neutrality.
3. **UK CIC (Community Interest Company)** with asset lock — closest UK analog.

Whichever form, the §5 sovereigns-vs-fiscal separation, the §6.2 funding guardrails, the §8 one-way ratchet, and the §9 dissolution priority are non-negotiable.
