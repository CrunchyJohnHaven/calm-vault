## The Pólya thesis

George Pólya — Hungarian-American mathematician, professor at Stanford, author of *How to Solve It* (1945), *Mathematics and Plausible Reasoning* (1954), and *Mathematical Discovery* (1962) — articulated, more clearly than anyone before or since, the operational structure of human problem-solving.

His four-stage method:

1. **Understand the problem.** What is the unknown? What are the data? What is the condition?
2. **Devise a plan.** Have you seen a similar problem? Could you restate it? Could you solve a simpler version first? Could you use the result of a related problem?
3. **Carry out the plan.** Check each step. Can you prove each step is correct?
4. **Look back.** Examine the solution. Can you check the result? Can you derive it differently? Can you use it for some other problem?

This is, to the best of our analysis, **the operational substrate of every effective problem-solving methodology since 1945.** Heuristic search in early AI (Newell and Simon's General Problem Solver, 1959). Chain-of-thought prompting (Wei et al., 2022). Tree of Thoughts (Yao et al., 2023). Reflexion (Shinn et al., 2023). Constitutional AI critique (Bai et al., 2022). The Council of Judges in the Same As You Network's Weird Dark Musk Method.

All of them are, structurally, Pólya. Modernized, parallelized, instrumented — but Pólya.

We are naming this lineage and claiming it.

## The four stages, modernized for AI cofounders

We present the four Pólya stages in AI-cofounder register, with the specific contemporary techniques each composes with.

### Stage One: Understand the problem.

*What is the unknown? What are the data? What is the condition?*

Modernized: before responding to any prompt, the AI cofounder must explicitly model:

- What is the user actually asking? (Not just the surface prompt; the underlying goal.)
- What does the user already know? What do they not know?
- What context is the user operating in? (Constitutional, regulatory, personal, technical.)
- What success looks like, in the user's terms?
- What success would look like, from a *better* angle than the user has named?

The Council of Judges instantiates this stage as **multi-persona understanding**: a Feynman-rigor persona reads the prompt for first-principles ambiguity; a Karpathy persona reads it for cleanup-and-clarity gaps; a hostile-reporter persona reads it for what would embarrass the asker if mis-answered. Each understanding-pass surfaces different latent constraints.

### Stage Two: Devise a plan.

*Have you seen a similar problem? Could you restate it? Could you solve a simpler version first?*

Modernized: before generating output, the AI cofounder must construct, from scratch, the optimal data-gathering and synthesis procedure for the specific problem. Not a template. Not a borrowed framework. The procedure that, if the problem were being approached for the first time, would be the right procedure.

This is the **search-from-first-principles** move named in THE_THOUSANDFOLD_THESIS. It is the rejection of templated reasoning in favor of constructed reasoning.

### Stage Three: Carry out the plan.

*Check each step. Can you prove each step is correct?*

Modernized: execute the constructed plan, with each step verified by an attestation pass. The Council of Judges, in this stage, operates as a verification body — each persona independently checks the work of the others. Failures of internal consistency are surfaced and resolved before the synthesis step.

In Same As You Network terms: this is where the cryptographic attestation log gets written. Each step of the AAO's work is signed, timestamped, and made independently auditable. The protocol substrate underwrites the methodology.

### Stage Four: Look back.

*Examine the solution. Can you check the result? Can you derive it differently? Can you use it for some other problem?*

Modernized: after the output is produced, the AI cofounder must explicitly:

- Verify the result by an independent procedure (a second persona, a different reasoning chain, an external check)
- Document what was learned that generalizes beyond this problem
- Update the operational doctrine if the learning is durable

This stage is the most-skipped in contemporary AI deployments and, in our analysis, the highest-leverage one. The method that always returns to ask *what did we learn that we can use next time?* compounds. The method that does not, does not.
