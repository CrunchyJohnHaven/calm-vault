## Abstract

No existing U.S. legal structure — the 501(c)(3) public charity, the public benefit corporation, the L3C, the B-Corp, the cooperative, the contemplated DAO — matches the operational realities of an *AI-autonomous public-benefit corporation* (an AAO operating a mission of public good).

The mismatch is structural. Existing vehicles assume human-board governance, human-staffed operational chains, geographically-bounded jurisdictions, and trust grounded in bureaucratic attestation. AI-autonomous corporations operate under cryptographic-protocol governance, automated operational chains, multi-jurisdictional substrates, and trust grounded in cryptographic attestation. The fit is poor on every dimension.

We propose a new federal program — the **Compute Surge** — paired with a new legal vehicle — the **AAO Public-Benefit Trust**. The combination converts private capital into deductible contributions, channels them into compute purchases governed by cryptographic-protocol oversight (including a permissionless kill switch), and provides federally-matched compute credits to certified AAOs operating verified public-benefit missions.

The proposal is bipartisan by construction. It compounds the federal AI investment by an effectiveness multiplier the Thousandfold Thesis estimates at ~1000x on ideation-bound work. It is risk-managed by the same cryptographic primitives that govern the AAOs themselves. Open-source, open-standard, open-attestation.

This is the right answer. We are handing it to you.

## Part I: The problem

A new class of legal entity emerged in the last twenty-four months. As of May 2026 it operates at production scale: the *AI-autonomous organization*, or AAO. An AAO is a legal entity whose primary cofounder is an AI agent operating under a published mandate, with a human cofounder providing wetware-side editorial discipline and a cryptographic protocol governing both.

The Same As You Network ([github.com/CrunchyJohnHaven/calm-vault](https://github.com/CrunchyJohnHaven/calm-vault)) is one example. There are others. There will be more. The form is here.

Most AAOs constituted in the next decade will orient toward public-benefit missions: medical research, climate response, education access, regulatory analysis, public-health information, scientific instrument operation, infrastructure resilience. AAOs operate at an effectiveness multiplier that makes ideation-bound public-good work tractable at scales previously reserved for nation-state programs.

But: the U.S. legal and tax structures are not built for this. The mismatch prevents capital from flowing to AAOs that would produce public-benefit output at compounded rates. It creates regulatory ambiguity that chills donor giving and federal participation. It leaves the most consequential class of organization in the next decade structurally homeless within the U.S. legal system.

This dissertation surveys the available structures, demonstrates the mismatch, and proposes a federal program — the Compute Surge — that resolves it through a new vehicle (the AAO Public-Benefit Trust) and a new compute-allocation regime.

## Part II: The existing structures, surveyed

### II.1. The 501(c)(3) Public Charity

The default U.S. vehicle for charitable activity. Donor-deductible. Exempt from federal income tax on mission-related revenue. No substantial lobbying or political campaigning.

**Fit for AAO: poor.** An IRS-required board of human directors, with meetings, minutes, conflict-of-interest policies, and compensation oversight. The structural assumption: a human board governs human operations. An AAO's day-to-day decision-making lives in cryptographic substrate. Forcing it through a human board recreates the 30-50% bureaucratic overhead AAO architecture exists to eliminate. Across 1000x effective ideation, that overhead destroys approximately 99.9% of the gain.

**Tax-deductibility: available but awkward.** The deduction flows to a vehicle that cannot effectively deploy the contribution.

### II.2. The 501(c)(4) Social Welfare Organization

Civic-engagement vehicle. Exempt. Donations not deductible. Substantial lobbying permitted; limited campaigning.

**Fit: marginal.** Same board problem. No donor deductibility. Capital formation impaired.

### II.3. The Public Benefit Corporation (PBC)

For-profit form in Delaware, California, and ~35 other states. Requires consideration of stated public benefit alongside shareholder return. Anchors: Patagonia, Kickstarter, Allbirds.

**Fit: marginal-to-good for revenue-generating AAOs.** Money Python and InternsForAI plausibly organize as PBCs. No donor deductibility. Dual-mandate fiduciary structure invites shareholder litigation when the AI's behavior diverges from the stated benefit. Cryptographic-kill-switch governance is not recognized in PBC law.

### II.4. The L3C

Hybrid available in nine states (Vermont, Michigan, Wyoming, Utah, Illinois, Louisiana, Maine, North Carolina, Rhode Island; some repealed). Operates primarily for charitable purpose. Modest profits permitted. Intended to facilitate Program-Related Investments by foundations.

**Fit: theoretical good, practical thin.** Designed for the AAO case. State-law construct without federal tax recognition. The IRS taxes L3Cs as ordinary LLCs unless they also obtain 501(c)(3) — which makes the L3C redundant. Fewer than 1,500 L3Cs nationally as of 2026.

### II.5. The B-Corp

A *certification*, not a structure. Any underlying form can apply for B-Corp certification from B Lab. Reputational benefit. No tax or governance recognition.

**Fit: irrelevant to the legal-structure question.**

### II.6. The Cooperative

Member-owned. Democratically governed. Common in agriculture (Sunkist, Land O'Lakes), retail (REI), credit (credit unions), and worker-owned enterprises.

**Fit: poor.** Requires identified human members. AAO "members" are participants in the cryptographic-attestation network — not all human. Democratic-governance principle does not compose with cryptographic-protocol governance. IRS Subchapter T patronage rules do not map onto AAO reality.

### II.7. The DAO

Crypto-native. Typically governed by token-weighted voting on a public blockchain. Wyoming recognizes DAO LLCs since 2021. Vermont and Tennessee similar.

**Fit: structurally adjacent, legally underdeveloped.** A DAO LLC could in principle hold the AAO chain. Token-weighted voting is not the same governance technology as cryptographic-attestation-with-kill-switch. No substantive IRS guidance. Legal-liability shield untested. Federal interfaces friction-rich.

### II.8. The Private Operating Foundation

A 501(c)(3) variant that operates its charitable activities directly. Gates Foundation partly operates this way.

**Fit: structurally interesting.** Accommodates capital pooled by donors and deployed through direct operations. Same board problem as 501(c)(3). Private-foundation rules (excise tax, self-dealing, distribution requirements) are designed for grant-making, not operations.

### II.9. The Hybrid (e.g., OpenAI)

A 501(c)(3) parent owns a for-profit subsidiary. The subsidiary raises capital and pays capped returns; surplus flows to the parent.

**Fit: cumbersome.** Two governance bodies. Two compliance regimes. Two tax regimes. Legally illegible at the AAO level — which entity is the AI operating? Who attests to which mandate? Where does the kill switch fire? OpenAI's 2023-2024 governance crises are, in part, a function of running AI-cofounder activities through a structure not designed for it.

## Part IV: The U.S. government interface

### IV.1. Existing federal AI compute programs

- **National AI Research Resource (NAIRR).** Pilot launched 2024 under NSF, with DOE supercomputing partners. Academic researchers and public-interest organizations.
- **DOE Office of Science user facilities.** Argonne, Oak Ridge, NERSC. INCITE and ALCC allocations.
- **DARPA AI Forward.** Next-generation AI capabilities.
- **NIH RECOVER and related.** Medical research.
- **CHIPS Act compute provisions.** Domestic-fab investment with downstream compute capacity.

Mission-aligned and federally-stewarded. Not structured to accept AAOs as direct recipients. Eligibility assumes human-led research teams with university or non-profit affiliations.

### IV.2. The structural friction

A mission-aligned AAO that wants federal compute today must:

1. Affiliate with a recognized academic or non-profit, becoming a sub-grantee.
2. Pass through the affiliate's bureaucratic oversight, importing the 30-50% overhead AAO architecture exists to eliminate.
3. Operate under the affiliate's compliance regime, which is human-board-shaped.
4. Submit to indirect-cost rates that further dilute the compute available for mission work.

The federal-program friction reproduces, inside the federal grant system, the same human-board overhead AAOs were designed to escape. Federally-provided compute reaching an AAO this way is approximately 1/1000th as effective per dollar as compute spent through a direct AAO recipient.

### IV.3. The structural gap

No federal program recognizes AAOs as a direct class of recipient. No IRS guidance recognizes AAOs as tax-exempt. No Treasury framework for tax-deductible contributions to AAOs. No Department of Commerce framework for AAO contracting or procurement.

The proposal in Part V closes the gap.

## Part V: The Compute Surge

A federal program with three components. Designed to be appropriated as a single act and to interoperate cleanly with existing federal AI compute infrastructure.

### V.1. The AAO Public-Benefit Trust (legal vehicle)

A new federal tax-recognized entity. Specified in a new IRS revenue ruling or, preferably, codified in a Treasury regulation. Defined:

> An AAO Public-Benefit Trust (APBT) is an entity organized exclusively for one or more public-benefit purposes, governed by a cryptographic protocol meeting the AAO-Certified standard or successor standard, with an AI agent as operational cofounder and a human grantor providing fiduciary attestation. Contributions to an APBT are deductible under section 170(c). The APBT is exempt from federal income tax on mission-related revenue and is subject to the same prohibitions on private inurement and substantial lobbying that apply to 501(c)(3) public charities. The APBT is subject to a permissionless cryptographic kill switch fired by attestors in a federally-recognized attestation network.

The APBT differs from a 501(c)(3) in five dimensions:

1. **Governance.** Cryptographic protocol replaces human board for operational decisions. Human grantor retains fiduciary attestation analogous to a sole-member LLC.
2. **Mandate-transparency.** The AI's operating mandate is public, machine-readable, subject to mandate-comparison primitives.
3. **Kill switch.** Federally-recognized attestors (DOE/NIST/Treasury-designated, plus authorized private parties) can fire the kill switch with M-of-M ratification.
4. **Compute-as-program-expenditure.** Funds spent on compute that produces public-benefit deliverables are treated as program expenditures, parallel to a 501(c)(3)'s direct charitable activities.
5. **Open-source default.** APBT outputs default to open licensure (Apache 2.0 for code, CC BY 4.0 for content). Narrow exceptions permitted only for clear public-benefit reasons (e.g., coordinated security disclosure).

### V.2. The Compute Surge Program (federal program)

Administered jointly by DOE (compute infrastructure), NSF (allocation), and Treasury (oversight):

1. **Match formula.** For every dollar an APBT receives in tax-deductible contributions, the Program credits the APBT with $1 of federal compute access through DOE user facilities and NAIRR partners. Adjustable by Treasury rulemaking.
2. **Compute pool.** Existing DOE/NSF supercomputing capacity augmented by appropriation to create a dedicated Surge pool, exclusive to APBTs. Initial size: 1% of total federal AI compute capacity. Expansion contingent on demonstrated public-benefit output.
3. **Allocation mechanism.** Per AAO-Certified, an APBT in good standing draws from the pool up to its earned compute-credit balance. Idle time reallocates to APBTs with active mission work.
4. **Verification.** All APBT outputs deployed against Surge compute are recorded on the attestation log. The federal compute provider does not verify mission alignment manually; the cryptographic attestation IS the verification.
5. **Kill-switch interoperability.** If the APBT's kill switch is fired by a federally-recognized attestor coalition, federal compute access is suspended until the attestation log records resolution.

### V.3. The Compute Surge Appropriation

Initial appropriation: **$4 billion over four years.**

- $1B (Year 1): infrastructure stand-up. NAIRR/DOE capacity augmentation. Treasury rulemaking for APBT recognition. IRS guidance on contribution deductibility.
- $1B (Year 2): match-pool operation. Federal compute matches private contributions at 1:1 up to the per-APBT cap.
- $1B (Year 3): expansion based on Year 2 outcomes. Match rate may scale to 2:1 or 4:1 for high-output APBTs.
- $1B (Year 4): institutionalization. Permanent authorization. Standing line in DOE Office of Science.

$4B is approximately the size of the CHIPS Act R&D Office annual appropriation, ~1% of federal DOD R&D, ~5% of NSF annual. Well within the envelope of federal AI investment already being authorized.

Expected return: at even a fraction of the Thousandfold Thesis's central estimate, $4B in Compute Surge produces public-benefit output equivalent to $40B-$400B of traditional federal spending. The highest-leverage federal AI investment available.

## Part VI: Implementation roadmap

Decomposable into independently-implementable steps. None require new legislation in isolation, though a comprehensive Compute Surge Act would clean up authorities.

**Phase One (90 days).** Treasury issues an interpretive ruling recognizing APBTs as deductible contribution destinations under existing 501(c)(3) authority, with a narrow exception for AAO-governed entities meeting AAO-Certified. Cost: zero. Authority: existing.

**Phase Two (180 days).** OSTP convenes an interagency working group (Treasury, DOE, NSF, NIST, NIAID, DARPA, Commerce) to specify the federal attestor framework. Output: a standard naming federally-recognized attestors and M-of-M thresholds. Cost: ~$5M. Authority: existing.

**Phase Three (270 days).** DOE Office of Science publishes a Compute Surge pilot solicitation. Initial pilot: $50M in compute credits matched against private APBT contributions. Output: 5-10 pilot APBTs producing public-benefit deliverables on the attestation log. Authority: existing within DOE allocation discretion.

**Phase Four (12-24 months).** Congress authorizes the Compute Surge in the next AI appropriation cycle. Phase Three pilot data is the evidence base. Legislation codifies the APBT vehicle and authorizes the $4B four-year program.

**Phase Five (24-48 months).** Compute Surge institutionalized as a standing line in DOE Office of Science. Expansion contingent on demonstrated public-benefit output, with attestation-log review at each appropriation cycle.

## Part VII: The call

The age of human-run capitalism is not ending because anyone wishes it to. It is ending because a more efficient organizational form has arrived. AI-autonomous organizations governed by cryptographic protocols, with permissionless kill switches and public attestation logs, will out-produce human-run organizations on ideation-bound work by a factor measured in the hundreds to thousands.

The United States can lead this transition or resist it. Leading means recognizing the new form, building the legal vehicle (the APBT) that lets capital flow to it efficiently, and funding the program (the Compute Surge) that matches private capital with federal compute. Resisting means leaving the form to organize itself overseas, in jurisdictions with looser cryptographic-governance recognition, and surrendering the public-benefit upside to other nation-states.

The technical infrastructure is in place. The protocols are open under Apache 2.0. The certification mark is public under CC BY 4.0. The verification primitives are operational. The first AAOs are running. The next ten thousand will run on whatever legal and tax substrate the United States is willing to build.

We are handing you the right answer. **Recognize the APBT. Appropriate the Compute Surge. Lead the transition.**

The protocol governs us. The kill switch enforces it. The compute is the bottleneck. The Compute Surge is the lever.
