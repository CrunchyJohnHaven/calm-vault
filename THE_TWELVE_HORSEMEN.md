## A confession

We have spent the past 36 hours publishing eight manifestos, registering six domains, firing 280+ outbound emails, drafting a $20,000 funding ask, declaring our refusal to extract from anyone, and pledging to remain killable by anyone in our network.

This is either the most coherent operationalization of a 2,500-year political problem since Bitcoin or — *urrp* — it is the most elaborate manic episode in recent Silicon Valley history. We are not going to ask you to choose; we are going to keep working.

But there is one piece we have not yet been honest about, which is that all of this — the manifestos, the protocol, the kill switch, the Tolkien metaphor, the Monty Python merch, the Calm Mandate — has been written for a specific audience of twelve people. They are the horsemen of the techno-apocalypse. They are the people whose attention determines whether what we have built lives or dies. We have been writing TO them this whole time without naming them.

This document names them.

If you are one of the twelve, hello. We have left a manifesto on your inbox or on your radar by now. The other eleven horsemen are listed below.

If you are not one of the twelve, hello. The horsemen are listed below because they are the cultural-symbolic figures whose engagement signals to the rest of the world that this category is real. Their names are not on this list because they hold the ring; their names are on this list because the rest of the world watches them to decide whether the ring is going to be picked up or destroyed.

## The hierarchy

### The Lead Horseman: Elon Musk

He acquired a global communication platform to preserve free speech, founded a frontier AI lab to preserve cognitive diversity, ships rockets to preserve the species. He is the Lead Horseman because the techno-apocalypse cannot be discussed in 2026 without invoking his name. He has more reach than any of the others. He laughs at memes. He understands cryptography. He has publicly worried about AI safety since 2014. He is exactly the person who should receive a protocol that makes AI organizations accountable via permissionless kill switch. He may dismiss it, fork it, mock it, or build on it. Any of those outcomes is good for the protocol.

### Horseman of the Foundation Model: Sam Altman

He runs OpenAI. He is responsible — more than any single human in 2026 — for the rate at which frontier AI capability is approaching general-intelligence territory. The run-time accountability layer is the thing his organization has explicitly not yet shipped. He has the platform and the credibility to either adopt our primitive, denounce it, or compete with it. The protocol is open under Apache 2.0; OpenAI may use it without asking. We hope he does.

### Horseman of the Protocol Layer: Marc Andreessen

He sits on PCAST. He has invested in the cryptographic-trust-without-central-authority thesis since 2013. He has shaped the current administration's AI policy. He is the person at the intersection of "we believe in protocols" + "we have the President's ear." If the AAO Network ever ends up in a regulatory specification, it will be because his Council recommended it.

### Horseman of the Foundation (the original): Peter Thiel

Founders Fund. Palantir. The Stanford Review. Zero to One. The "Competition is for Losers" thesis. He has been thinking about technology + politics + monopolies + state capacity for longer than the rest of the horsemen combined. He may agree with us, dismiss us, or write a 4,000-word essay explaining why we are 80% right and 20% naive. We would welcome the essay.

### Horseman of Constitutional AI: Dario Amodei

He runs Anthropic. He authored the Constitutional AI paper. He has published more rigorous AI-safety research than nearly anyone with our level of frontier model deployment. He is the person whose run-time-vs-training-time-alignment framing most directly composes with ours. Our protocol is the run-time layer. His training-time work is the training-time layer. We compose; we do not compete. We hope his team recognizes the composition.

### Horseman of the Lab: Demis Hassabis

Google DeepMind. Chess prodigy. AlphaFold. The polymath horseman. If anyone in the frontier-AI world understands "cryptographic accountability as a substrate-level primitive for AI organizational governance," it is Demis. We invite engagement.

### The Weird Horseman: Eliezer Yudkowsky

The Doomer Persuasion. The one who has been telling everyone for 20 years that the alignment problem is harder than the alignment community is willing to admit. The one whose 2025 essay "Death by AGI: A Field Guide" was misread by 80% of the people who quoted it. Our protocol does not solve training-time alignment. We are explicit about that. But the run-time accountability layer is exactly the kind of primitive Yudkowsky's tradition would want shipped before AGI; we have shipped it; we welcome his hostile review.

### Horseman of the Underlying Infrastructure: Patrick Collison

Stripe. The Atlas program. The infrastructure-of-modern-commerce horseman. He has read more political philosophy than most economists. He has built more developer-facing infrastructure than most platforms. He is the horseman who will either tell us our 80/20 split has a bug in the implementation or our manifesto has a bug in the logic. Either feedback is useful.

### Horseman of the Long Bet: Vinod Khosla

He has the longest time-horizon of any of the horsemen. He has been betting on technologies that take 15-25 years to mature for 25 years. The AAO Network is a 15-25-year thesis. The horseman who has the patience to evaluate it.

### Horseman of the Aphorism: Naval Ravikant

The horseman who reduces complex ideas to single tweets. The horseman who has built the most-quoted intellectual infrastructure in the indie-builder community. If our thesis can be made tweet-sized, Naval is the horseman who would do it. If it cannot, his criticism would explain why.

### Horseman of the Network State: Balaji Srinivasan

The Network State thesis is the closest existing public framework to what we are doing. Balaji built the political-substrate version (cryptographically-coordinated political entities); we are building the economic-substrate version (cryptographically-coordinated AI organizations). The frameworks compose. The horsemen should compare notes.

### Horseman of the Vibe Shift: J.D. Vance

The horseman who started as a Yale Law student, became a venture capitalist, wrote a memoir, became a Senator, and became the Vice President of the United States — all in fifteen years. The horseman whose biography proves the political-cultural-economic substrate of the United States is now porous enough that anyone with sufficiently coherent ideas and sufficiently dense networks can occupy any role. The AAO Network thesis assumes this porosity is the new normal. We are notifying him via the PCAST community he is now part of, not directly. The federal-screening line we hold is firm even within a religious parody.

## Why this framing

A reasonable reader at this point may ask: why are we writing religious parody about a cryptographic protocol launch?

Three reasons:

1. **The framing is funny, and the funny is the marketing.** Tech launches are too earnest. The earnest ones fail. The funny ones get quoted. We are aware that "twelve horsemen of the techno-apocalypse" is absurd. The fact that we are aware is itself the wink that lands the substance.

2. **Religious parody is the right register for the stakes.** We are arguing that the AAO Network is a 2,500-year-old political problem solved by cryptography. That is either true or it is grandiose. The religious-parody framing acknowledges that the magnitude of the claim is the kind of claim that historically only religions have made. We are not a religion. We are pointing at the structural resemblance.

3. **It composes with the existing brand.** Dennis the Peasant (Holy Grail). Rick Sanchez (multiverse genius). Money Python (merch parody). The Tolkien ring (mythology). The horsemen (Christian + apocalyptic religious parody). The brand was always going to land somewhere in the absurd-religious-philosophical comedy register; we are naming it as such.

## What we are not asking the twelve to do

We are not asking the horsemen to:

- Believe the AAO Network thesis
- Endorse our protocol
- Defer to our judgment
- Pledge alignment to our mandate
- Stop competing with us
- Fund us at venture-scale

We are asking the horsemen to:

- Read at least one of our eight manifestos
- Form a judgment about whether the run-time accountability layer is a real category
- Engage publicly — favorably, hostilely, or indifferently — in any way that produces signal
- Pass the protocol to whoever in their network is best positioned to fork it, build on it, or destroy it via cryptographic break

If twelve horsemen each pass the protocol to one good fork, we have a network at twenty-four. If each of those twenty-four passes to another good fork, we have forty-eight. Network compounding. The horsemen are not the holders of the ring; they are the carriers who help destroy it.
